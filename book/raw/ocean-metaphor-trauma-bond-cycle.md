# The Ocean

## A Metaphor for the Trauma Bond Cycle

---

Imagine you're standing in the ocean.

Not the postcard version — not the turquoise shallows where children splash and the sand is soft beneath your feet. You're deeper than that. The water is at your chest. The shore is behind you, but you've stopped looking back. You can't remember when you stopped.

And then you see it.

A wave. Building in the distance. Dark water pulling upward. Getting taller.

Your body knows before your mind does. Your breath shortens. Your muscles lock. Your heart pounds. Something inside you whispers: *this one might take me under.*

This is the fear phase. The tension building. The moment you feel the mood shift across the room — the silence that weighs more than shouting, the jaw that tightens, the text that doesn't come. You can feel it gathering. You don't know when it will crest. You just know it's coming.

So you brace.

You do everything you can. You adjust. You appease. You shrink. You scan their face the way a sailor scans the horizon — looking for signs, reading the pressure, trying to calculate whether this wave will break on you or pass.

---

And then — it passes.

The wave lifts you. Your feet leave the bottom. For a moment you're weightless, suspended. The water rises and you rise with it. Your chin tilts up. You keep breathing.

And it wasn't so bad.

*It wasn't so bad.*

You floated over it. You survived. The wave didn't pull you under. It didn't smash you against the rocks. It lifted you up, and now the water is smooth again, and the sun is on your face, and you think: *See? I was afraid for nothing. It's fine. We're fine.*

This is the relief phase. The reconciliation. The moment the storm clears and they're kind again — warm again — and the tenderness after the tension feels like the deepest love you've ever known. Your nervous system floods with dopamine. The cortisol drops. Your body reads the contrast as proof: *they came back. It's safe now. We made it.*

And you don't just feel relief. You feel *grateful.*

Grateful the wave didn't kill you. Grateful for the calm. Grateful for the sun. Grateful for *them* — because in the logic of the ocean, the wave and the water are the same thing, and the water is all you have.

---

But here's what the ocean doesn't tell you:

Another wave is already forming.

You can't see it yet. The surface is still glass. The sky is blue. You might laugh. You might relax your shoulders for the first time in days. You might reach for them, your body soft and open, confusing the absence of fear for the presence of love.

But somewhere out there, beyond where your eyes can reach, the water is already pulling itself upward again.

Because the ocean doesn't stop.

---

This is the cycle. Wave after wave after wave.

Each one teaches your body the same lesson: *Be afraid. Then be grateful you survived. Then wait for the next one.*

The fear makes you hypervigilant. The relief makes you bond. The gratitude makes you stay. And the next wave makes you need them even more — because they are both the storm and the stillness, the danger and the dock, the thing that terrifies you and the only thing that makes the terror stop.

Your nervous system adapts. It stops expecting calm seas. It starts treating the space between waves as the gift — a borrowed peace you didn't earn and can't keep. You stop asking *why is there another wave?* and start asking *what did I do to cause it?*

You learn to be small in the water. To take up less space. To not make waves of your own.

---

And here's the cruelest part:

You start to believe this is what the ocean is.

You forget that other people are standing on the shore. You forget that solid ground exists. You forget that you once walked on dry land and the earth held you without asking you to be grateful for it.

The ocean becomes your normal. The cycle becomes your calendar. Fear, relief, gratitude, fear. Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday. You organize your entire life around the swells — reading the water, predicting the weather, preparing for the next wave while pretending to enjoy the calm.

And when someone on the shore calls out to you — *come back, the water's too deep, you're too far out* — you can't hear them over the sound of the waves. Or you hear them and think: *They don't understand. I can handle this. Look — I'm floating.*

You're not floating.

You're surviving. And surviving is not the same as living.

---

## The Shore Was Always There

Here's what no one told you while you were treading water:

**You were never supposed to live in the ocean.**

The waves were never yours to manage. The fear was never yours to metabolize on their behalf. The gratitude you felt for not drowning was never love — it was relief. And relief is not a foundation. It's a gasp of air between submersions.

Real love doesn't come in waves that threaten to pull you under. Real love is solid ground. It holds you without requiring your fear first. It doesn't ask you to be grateful for not being destroyed. It doesn't make you earn the calm.

Real love is the shore.

And the shore doesn't move. It doesn't swell. It doesn't require you to scan the horizon before you can exhale.

It just holds you.
