# Raw: The Go-Between — When Divorce Made Me the Messenger

**Date:** February 18, 2026 (updated February 21, 2026)
**Topic:** Parentification through divorce — being used as a communication conduit between parents; enmeshment, projection, and programmatic relating
**Related:** Vol 1 Ch 7 (Triangulation, Golden Child), Vol 1 Ch 9 (Childhood Patterns), Vol 2 Ch 4 (Parentified Child), Vol 3 (Individuation as Betrayal), Vol 5 Ch 10 (Child Mirrors Wound), Vol 5 Ch 11 (Co-Parenting), Vol 5 Ch 12 (When Grandparents Are the Problem)

---

## The Experience

My parents after divorce wouldn't speak and used me as a go-between to converse about everything from money to schedules.

---

## What This Actually Was

This is one of the clearest forms of **post-divorce parentification through triangulation** — not the dramatic kind where a child is weaponized or turned against a parent, but the quiet, functional kind where a child becomes the family's communication infrastructure.

When two adults refuse to speak to each other but still need to coordinate a shared life — finances, schedules, holidays, logistics — someone has to carry those messages. And when the only person both parents will talk to is their child, that child becomes a living telephone line between two people who chose silence over discomfort.

---

## The Mechanics

**What it looked like from the outside:**
- "Tell your father the check is late."
- "Ask your mom what time she's picking you up Friday."
- "Let your dad know I changed the insurance."
- "Can you find out from your mother if she's taking you for Christmas?"

**What it felt like on the inside:**
- Every message carried weight it shouldn't have
- The money conversations were the worst — carrying financial tension between two adults who refused to negotiate directly
- Schedule coordination meant being responsible for logistics no child should manage
- Every relay was a loyalty test disguised as a practical request
- You become hypervigilant about tone — *how* to deliver the message so it doesn't start a fight you'll be in the middle of
- You learn to edit, soften, translate — becoming a diplomat before you understand what diplomacy costs

---

## The Invisible Promotion

No one sits you down and says, "You are now the family mediator." It happens gradually:

1. First, they stop talking to each other
2. Then, small requests: "Just mention to your dad..."
3. Then, bigger ones: "Tell your mother I can't do the 15th..."
4. Then, financial ones: "Ask your father about the support payment..."
5. Then, it's just how things work — you are the channel

By the time you realize what's happened, it's been your job for years. You didn't apply for it. You didn't consent to it. But stopping feels like you'd be the one breaking the family.

---

## What It Teaches You (That You Have to Unlearn)

- **That you are responsible for other people's communication** — If you don't carry the message, it doesn't get delivered. The system depends on you.
- **That direct conflict is so dangerous even adults can't do it** — If your parents can't talk to each other, what hope do you have of ever being direct?
- **That your needs come last** — The message is always more important than how carrying it makes you feel.
- **That love means being useful** — You matter because you're the bridge. Without the function, who are you?
- **That you must be neutral at all costs** — Taking sides means losing a parent. So you learn to flatten yourself.
- **That money is a weapon or a wound** — Carrying financial messages between parents teaches you that money is never just money. It's leverage, resentment, control, and pain.

---

## The Adult Pattern

Children who served as the family go-between often become adults who:

- Over-function in communication — managing relationships between others, translating, softening, bridging
- Feel anxious when people around them aren't communicating
- Automatically step into mediator roles at work, in friendships, in partnerships
- Struggle to stay out of conflicts that aren't theirs
- Have complicated relationships with money that echo the financial messages they carried
- Feel responsible when relationships around them fail ("If I had just said it differently...")
- Find rest threatening — because when you stop bridging, the system collapses
- Choose partners who need them to manage communication with others (recreating the role)

---

## The Decoder

> "I was not a messenger. I was a child who deserved parents who could speak to each other."

> "Their silence was not my responsibility to fill."

> "Carrying messages about money between two adults was never my job — and the weight of it shaped how I relate to money, conflict, and my own worth."

# Raw: The Friend She Wanted — When Your Mother Makes You Her Mirror

**Date:** February 21, 2026
**Topic:** Enmeshment through friendship — when a mother treats her child as confidant, projects her unlived life, and reads individuation as betrayal
**Related:** Vol 1 Ch 7 (Golden Child / Narcissistic Extension), Vol 1 Ch 9 (Childhood Patterns — Conditional Love), Vol 3 (Standing — Individuation), Vol 5 Ch 10 (Child Mirrors Wound), Vol 5 Ch 12 (When Grandparents Are the Problem)

---

## The Experience

My mom thought of me as her friend. She would confide in me, and she thought I was like her — and wanted me to do the same things she had wanted to do. So me going my own way was somehow a slight to her.

I always feel when I talk to her she's more interested in finding issues and wanting to know about my finances. Her unease she both hides and projects through her line of questioning — not actually present in a conversation, more running on a program.

---

## What This Actually Was

This is **enmeshment through false friendship** — a form of parentification where the parent doesn't just lean on the child for emotional support but collapses the generational boundary entirely. The child becomes the peer, the mirror, the confidant. And inside that "closeness" lives a set of expectations that are invisible until you violate them.

When a mother sees her child as a version of herself — as someone who shares her desires, her values, her unlived ambitions — the child's separate identity becomes a problem. Not because the child did anything wrong, but because differentiation breaks the mirror. If I am not like you, who are you seeing when you look at me? And who were you seeing before?

**The friendship was never between equals.** A parent who confides in a child is not sharing — they are outsourcing. A parent who expects their child to want what they wanted is not relating — they are extending. The child carries the weight of the parent's emotional life and unlived dreams without ever being asked.

---

## The Mechanics

**What it looked like from the outside:**
- A "close" mother-daughter relationship
- A mother who "told her everything"
- A child who seemed mature, wise beyond her years
- A bond others might have envied

**What it actually was:**
- The child held the mother's confidences before she could hold her own
- The mother's dreams were installed as the child's aspirations
- Agreement was closeness; divergence was betrayal
- The child's own desires were invisible or threatening

**The programming:**
- "We're the same, you and me"
- "I just want for you what I wanted for myself"
- "After everything I've shared with you..."
- The unspoken: *your separate life is a rejection of mine*

---

## The Line of Questioning — Running on a Program

There is a particular quality to conversations with a parent who relates through enmeshment. The conversation is not a conversation. It's a scan.

**What it looks like:**
- Questions that probe for problems rather than connect
- A focus on finances — not out of care, but out of unease
- An undercurrent of looking for something wrong
- The feeling that you are being assessed, not heard

**What it feels like:**
- You're on the other end of an interrogation disguised as concern
- She's not actually present — she's executing a pattern
- Her anxiety runs the conversation, and her questions are the vehicle
- You can feel the unease she's hiding underneath the questions she's asking
- There's no room for you in the exchange because she's already occupied with her own projected fears

**The program:**
- Step 1: Ask about life (surface)
- Step 2: Probe for problems (the real objective)
- Step 3: Focus on finances (where anxiety concentrates)
- Step 4: Project unease through concern (disguised control)
- Step 5: Register any independence as a data point of worry

She is not in the conversation. She is running the conversation. There is a difference, and you can feel it — even when you can't name it.

---

## The Individuation Wound

When a mother has made you her mirror, your own path becomes a crack in her reflection.

**What individuation looks like to the enmeshed parent:**
- You choosing differently = "She doesn't value what I value"
- You moving away = "She's abandoning me"
- You succeeding on your terms = "She thinks she's better than me"
- You not needing her = "I have no role anymore"

**What individuation costs the child:**
- Guilt for having your own life
- The persistent feeling that being yourself hurts someone
- Difficulty trusting your own desires (are these mine or hers?)
- A relationship that gets smaller every time you get bigger

The slight she feels is not about what you did. It's about what your separateness means for her identity. If you were her friend, her mirror, her continuation — then your departure is not growth. It's abandonment. And she'll let you know through every probing question, every focus on your finances, every scan for cracks.

---

## The "I Had a Friend" Field Note Style

> I had a friend whose mother treated her like a best friend. Told her everything — the marriage problems, the money worries, the dreams she never followed. My friend grew up feeling special, chosen, trusted with adult things. She didn't realize until much later that she'd been carrying her mother's emotional life since she was nine.
>
> When she moved to a different country, her mother didn't say she was proud. She said, "I never thought you'd leave me." When she chose a career her mother didn't understand, the conversations shifted. Suddenly every phone call was an audit. How's the money? Are you sure about this? Have you thought about what could go wrong? Her mother called it caring. My friend called it suffocating. Neither of them was entirely wrong.
>
> The hardest part wasn't the questions. It was realizing her mother had never once asked her what she actually wanted. Every question was a search for confirmation that the daughter's life validated the mother's choices — or a probe for evidence that the departure was a mistake. Not a conversation. A program. Running the same subroutine every time: scan for problems, focus on money, project unease, register distance.
>
> My friend said, "She's not talking to me. She's talking to the version of me she needs to exist."

---

## The Decoder

> "Her friendship was not friendship. It was a mirror she needed me to hold."

> "My own path is not a betrayal of hers. It is the whole point of having a life."

> "When she asks about my finances, she is not asking about money. She is asking whether my independence is safe for her."

> "I can feel when someone is present in a conversation and when they are running a program. I trust that feeling now."
