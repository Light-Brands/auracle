# Appendix A: Diagnostic Pattern Map

## A Self-Assessment for Any System You Are Inside or Considering

---

> *"Pattern literacy is how people stop repeating history."*

---

This appendix is a diagnostic. It is not a verdict.

The questions below are designed to be asked of any organization, workplace, community, or relationship-shaped system you are participating in or considering joining. They map directly to the patterns developed in the chapters of this book. Some questions will land lightly. Others will land harder. Most readers find that one or two questions surface immediately as already-known truths the body had filed but not yet spoken.

Answer honestly. The diagnostic does not work if you are answering for the version of the system the system would prefer you to see. Answer for the version your body is registering.

---

## The Fifteen Questions

### 1. Are written agreements in place that name scope, decisions, money, and exit?

Look for actual documents. Signed contracts, role descriptions, compensation schedules, partnership agreements. Not promises to write things down later. Not gestures at structure. Actual paper, actual signatures, actual specificity.

*Maps to: Chapter 6 (Infrastructure as Control), Chapter 16 (Rebuilding Without Repeating)*

---

### 2. When you ask about money, does the warmth in the room remain steady?

The single most reliable diagnostic. Ask a direct, kind question about compensation, payment timing, or commission structure. Watch what happens to the leader's tone, the temperature of the room, the speed of the next response. If the warmth shifts, the system is operating on emotional compensation as a control mechanism.

*Maps to: Chapter 10 (Financial Ambiguity as Leverage), Pattern 7 (Emotional Compensation Loop)*

---

### 3. Is feedback specific enough to act on?

Can you reduce the most recent piece of feedback to a particular page, deliverable, or action that, if completed, would resolve the note? Or does the feedback float in conceptual language — alignment, refinement, coherence, precision — without operational specificity?

*Maps to: Chapter 11 (Mentorship That Becomes Control), Pattern 10 (Benevolent Authority Framing)*

---

### 4. Does inquiry produce information, or punishment?

When someone in the system asks the inconvenient question — about authority, about money, about the gap between what is said and what is done — what is the next twenty-four hours like for them? Do they receive an answer, or do they experience a subtle shift in their access, their assignments, or the leader's warmth?

*Maps to: Chapter 4 (The System Signature), Pattern 4 (Retaliation as Silencing Technology)*

---

### 5. Is there a Stabilizer in the system — someone empowered to translate vision into agreements, separate from the visionary?

If the only person empowered to write things down and make them stick is the same person whose authority is being formalized, the system is personality-driven. The Stabilizer role is the structural feature that prevents drift into implicit hierarchy.

*Maps to: Chapter 11 (Mentorship That Becomes Control), Chapter 16 (Rebuilding Without Repeating)*

---

### 6. Do roles have names, or are they assumed?

Can you say, in one sentence, what your role is? Can the leader? Can the other members? Or are you operating in a haze of *we all just contribute what we can*, in which the actual hierarchy is unwritten but felt?

*Maps to: Chapter 9 (Hierarchy & Rank in Mission-Driven Systems)*

---

### 7. Has compensation been specified before warmth deepened?

Before the relationship became emotionally heavy — before you became *part of the family*, before you were folded into the inner circle, before significant work was performed — was the structure of payment, equity, ownership, or exchange written down?

*Maps to: Chapter 10 (Financial Ambiguity as Leverage), Pattern 8 (Premature Bonding)*

---

### 8. Are there self-assigned titles, ranks, or co-founder claims that are not held by structural agreement?

Has anyone in the system named themselves with elevated language — *co-founder, second only to the founder, lead architect, senior teacher* — that is not reflected in a formal document signed by the relevant parties? Self-assigned rank is a structural red flag regardless of how it is justified.

*Maps to: Chapter 9 (Hierarchy & Rank in Mission-Driven Systems), Strategy Document Analysis (Appendix B)*

---

### 9. Is the leader present in the work they claim authority over?

Does the leader show up in the day-to-day life of the system — the class, the work, the meetings, the operations — or do they direct the system from a distance while remaining largely absent from its core activity? Authority claimed without presence is structural, not earned.

*Maps to: Chapter 5 (Platform Shielding), Chapter 11 (Mentorship That Becomes Control)*

---

### 10. When concerns are raised, are they addressed structurally or pathologized personally?

If you raise a concern about a pattern in the system, is the response a structural conversation about the pattern? Or is the response a reframing of you — *you have issues with men, you have issues with authority, you are projecting your wounding, you are not yet ready to receive feedback*?

*Maps to: Pattern 4 (Retaliation as Silencing Technology), Magician Tactics: The Pathologization (Appendix B)*

---

### 11. Does the system run on centralized access — portal, leads, communication channels, financial flows — controlled by one person?

Are the tools you depend on to do the work owned by the person you would need to report? Is there a parallel route to compensation, customers, students, donors, or community? Or does everything flow through one node?

*Maps to: Chapter 6 (Infrastructure as Control)*

---

### 12. Do exits in this system come with explanation, or with silence and language wrapped around the absence?

When people leave, what does the leaving look like? Is there an honest conversation about what didn't work? Or do exits arrive as gratitude messages, soft apologies *to anyone who was hurt*, and spiritual completion language that never names what actually happened?

*Maps to: Chapter 15 (The Exit), Vignette 8 (The Message That Didn't Say It)*

---

### 13. Does your body relax or tighten when you think about the system?

Set the analysis aside for a moment. Picture the leader. Picture the next meeting. Picture the next interaction. Where is the tension in your body? What is your breath doing? Your nervous system has been running this diagnostic in the background for longer than your mind has had words for it.

*Maps to: Chapter 12 (Neuroception in Manipulative Systems), Chapter 13 (Somatic Recognition)*

---

### 14. If nothing changed in this system for another year, would you feel relieved or depleted?

The question that bypasses hope. Not *will it get better* but *if this is the steady state, what is your body's verdict on the steady state*? Hope is a useful emotion, but it is not a diagnostic. The body knows what staying-as-is would cost.

*Maps to: Pattern 9 (Emotional Spillover + Relational Pull)*

---

### 15. Is there anyone you can compare notes with, or have you been kept structurally isolated from the others?

Do you know what other contributors are paid, promised, or asked to do? Do you have relationships with peers in the system that exist outside the leader's mediation? Or is your experience of the system funneled through the leader, leaving you unable to compare notes with anyone in a similar position?

*Maps to: Chapter 7 (Collapse Through Connection)*

---

## How to Interpret Your Answers

This is not a numerical scoring system. It is a qualitative reading.

**If three or more of these questions return answers that concern you, the system has a structural problem.** That is true regardless of how warm the leader is, how aligned the mission feels, or how genuine the relationships seem. Structural problems do not require malicious intent to produce harm. They require only that the structure remain in place.

**If five or more return concerning answers, the system is operating on the four-step infrastructure pattern this book is built around.** Centralized access, manufactured ambiguity, consequences for inquiry, restored control through gifts. The patterns are running. They will continue to run. The question for you is no longer *is this happening* but *what am I going to do about it*.

**If seven or more return concerning answers, your body has likely already filed for departure.** Listen to it. You do not have to leave today, but you should stop deepening today. Stop adding scope. Stop volunteering for additional responsibility. Stop letting the relationship escalate past the point at which the structure has caught up. Hold the line you are at and watch.

**If a single question returns an answer that hits especially hard — if reading it produced a sudden inner clarity, a chest tightening, a moment of *yes, that's the one* — trust that signal.** The diagnostic is not really fifteen questions. It is one question for each reader, and the others are scaffolding. Find your one. Sit with it. The rest of the chapters in this book are commentary on what your body just told you.

## Integration

The diagnostic is meant to be returned to. Some readers will run it once, recognize what they need to recognize, and act on it. Others will run it monthly, watching whether a system they are uncertain about is improving, holding steady, or drifting deeper into the four-step pattern.

The questions are also useful in reverse. If you are building a system, run the diagnostic on the thing you are building. Aim for fifteen answers that would not concern an honest reader. The structural commitments in Chapter 16 are the operational version of what passing this diagnostic looks like.

The point of the diagnostic is not paranoia. It is calm. The body that knows where to look is a body that does not have to scan for threat all day. The body that has criteria can return its attention to the work, the people, the meaning.

That is the gift of pattern literacy. The vigilance becomes lighter, not heavier, once it has somewhere specific to land.
