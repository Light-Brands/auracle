# Chapter 22: Working It Out

## Staying and Doing the Work — Across Every Kind of Committed Relationship

---

There comes a moment in every meaningful relationship when leaving would be easier.

When the fight has been going on too long. When the same wound keeps getting poked. When the distance between you feels wider than your will to cross it.

And yet — you stay.

Not because you're trapped. Not because you're afraid. Not because of guilt or obligation or habit.

**Because you've chosen this person.**

This chapter is about what happens after that choice. How to actually work through the hard parts — not just in romantic partnership, but in friendships, chosen family, and spiritual bonds. Because commitment doesn't eliminate conflict. It gives conflict a container strong enough to hold it.

---

## Field Note: The Friendship That Almost Ended

They'd been close for nine years.

Then came the thing that happens in deep friendships: one of them changed. Started a new spiritual practice. Started speaking differently. Started canceling plans to go on retreats.

The other felt abandoned. Left behind. Judged.

They didn't talk about it. For months.

Then one night, a text: "I miss you. And I'm angry at you. Can we talk?"

They sat together. It was awkward. It was honest. Neither apologized for who they were becoming. Both acknowledged the distance. Both said they wanted to stay.

That conversation didn't fix everything. But it turned a drift into a dialogue. The friendship didn't go back to what it was. It became something neither of them expected — deeper, more honest, less frequent, more real.

**They worked it out by refusing to pretend it wasn't happening.**

---

## The Decision to Stay

Working it out begins with a conscious decision.

Not a desperate one. Not a resigned one. A sovereign one.

**"I choose this relationship. Not because it's easy. Not because I have to. Because this person matters to me, and I'm willing to do the work."**

This decision changes everything — not because it eliminates difficulty, but because it provides the ground underneath difficulty. When you know you're staying, you can afford to be honest. You can afford to be uncomfortable. You can afford to disagree without the terror that disagreement means the end.

**Without the decision to stay, conflict is existential.** Every fight becomes a referendum on the relationship itself.

**With the decision to stay, conflict becomes navigable.** It's no longer "Are we going to survive this?" It's "How do we get through this together?"

---

## What "Working It Out" Is Not

Before going further, some clarity.

**Working it out is not:**

- Tolerating abuse because you've "committed"
- Losing yourself to maintain the connection
- Performing repair while seething underneath
- Agreeing to keep the peace
- Abandoning your sovereignty for the relationship
- Staying because leaving would be harder
- Confusing loyalty with self-abandonment

**Working it out IS:**

- Two sovereign people choosing to navigate difficulty together
- Holding your position while staying in relationship
- Being willing to be uncomfortable for something that matters
- Speaking truth even when it's hard, because the relationship can hold it
- Allowing the relationship to change form while maintaining the bond
- Doing your own work so you can show up to the shared work

**If staying requires you to abandon yourself, it isn't commitment. It's captivity.**

The distinction is critical. Everything in this chapter assumes you've already established that this relationship is worth your sovereignty — not despite it.

---

## Why Different Relationships Require Different Skills

We tend to treat all relationships as if they follow the same rules.

They don't.

How you work through conflict with a lifelong friend is not the same as how you work through conflict with a lover. How you navigate disagreement with chosen family is not the same as how you navigate it with biological family you've chosen to maintain.

Each relationship type has:
- **Different attachment patterns** activated
- **Different power dynamics** at play
- **Different cultural scripts** influencing expectations
- **Different stakes** when things go wrong
- **Different repair pathways** that actually work

Understanding these differences is the difference between repair that lands and repair that misses.

## Working It Out With Friends

### The Specific Challenge

Friendships are the least-scripted relationships.

There are no vows. No legal contracts. No biological ties. No cultural ceremonies marking their significance. A friendship can end with a slow fade, and no one will ask what happened.

This lack of structure is both the beauty and the vulnerability of friendship. There's nothing holding you there except choice. Which means working it out requires you to consciously choose — again and again — something the culture doesn't require of you.

### What Conflict Looks Like in Friendship

Friendship conflict is often invisible. It rarely explodes. It erodes.

**Common patterns:**

- **The Slow Fade:** One person pulls back. The other doesn't know why. Neither addresses it. The friendship dies of silence.

- **The Unspoken Resentment:** One person feels they give more. The imbalance isn't discussed. Bitterness builds until the friendship feels heavy.

- **The Growth Gap:** One person changes — new values, new lifestyle, new language. The other feels left behind or judged. Neither names it.

- **The Loyalty Test:** A crisis reveals mismatched expectations. One person needed more than the other could give. Disappointment becomes distance.

- **The Comparison Trap:** Success, relationships, children, career — life milestones create unspoken competition. Celebration becomes contaminated with envy.

### How to Work It Out

**1. Name the awkward thing.**

Friendships die from what goes unsaid. The repair begins when someone is brave enough to say: "Something feels off between us. Can we talk about it?"

This sentence is harder in friendship than in any other relationship — because there's no obligation to have the conversation. Choosing to have it is an act of commitment.

**2. Drop the performance of "fine."**

Friends often perform ease. "It's fine." "No worries." "All good."

Working it out requires stopping the performance. "Actually, it's not fine. I felt hurt when you canceled on me three times. I need you to know that."

**3. Allow asymmetry.**

Friendships don't need to be perfectly balanced at every moment. But they need to be reciprocal over time. If you're always the one reaching out, always the one listening, always the one adjusting — name it.

"I notice I'm usually the one initiating. I need that to go both ways."

**4. Renegotiate without ending.**

Friendships change. The friend you partied with at twenty may not be the friend you need at forty. That doesn't mean the friendship has to end. It means the terms need updating.

"I can't do every-weekend hangouts anymore, but I still want to be in your life. What would work for both of us?"

**5. Let them be where they are.**

Your friend may not be doing the work you're doing. They may not have the language you have. Working it out sometimes means accepting the friendship at the level they can meet you — without pretending it's something it's not.

### A Practice: The Friend Repair Conversation

Use this framework when something needs addressing:

- **Open:** "I value this friendship, and something's been bothering me. Can we talk?"
- **Name:** "When [specific thing happened], I felt [emotion]."
- **Listen:** "What's been going on for you?"
- **Acknowledge:** "I hear that. I can see how that would feel."
- **Negotiate:** "What would help? What do we both need going forward?"
- **Reconnect:** "I'm glad we talked. You matter to me."

### Field Note: The Friend Who Said the Hard Thing

She finally said it. After two years of avoiding it.

"I feel like you only call when you need something. I love being your support, but I need you to show up for my life too."

Her friend got quiet. Then defensive. Then quiet again.

A week later, her friend sent a long text. Not defensive. Just honest: "You're right. I didn't realize I was doing that. I'm sorry. I want to do better."

The friendship didn't transform overnight. But something shifted. The unspoken became spoken. The resentment had somewhere to go. And both of them were brave enough to keep going.

## Working It Out With a Lover or Partner

### The Specific Challenge

Romantic relationships carry the highest attachment charge. The nervous system is most activated here. The stakes feel survival-level even when they're not.

Volume 3, Chapter 15 explores sovereignty in partnership extensively. This section builds on that foundation with specific "working it out" practices — what to do when you've decided to stay and the work is in front of you.

### What Makes Lover Conflict Different

- **The attachment system is fully online.** Every disagreement can trigger abandonment or engulfment fears.
- **The body remembers before the mind thinks.** Implicit memory from early attachment activates in intimate conflict.
- **Sex and vulnerability are intertwined.** Conflict affects physical intimacy, which affects emotional connection, which affects conflict. The loop is tight.
- **You live together (often).** There's no physical distance to reset. You share space, money, decisions, children. The stakes are logistical as well as emotional.

### How to Fight Without Destroying

**1. Regulate before you engage.**

The single most important skill. If your nervous system is flooded, you will say things from your amygdala, not from yourself. Take the pause. Even if they don't want you to.

"I need twenty minutes. I'm not leaving. I'm regulating so I can actually be present for this."

Set a timer if needed. Come back. The break is not avoidance — it's preparation.

**2. Fight the problem, not each other.**

The shift: from "You vs. Me" to "Us vs. The Problem."

Instead of: "You never listen to me."
Try: "We have a listening problem. What's getting in the way?"

Instead of: "You always spend too much."
Try: "We need to figure out our money stuff. What do we each need?"

**3. One issue at a time.**

Couple fights escalate when old grievances pile on. The kitchen-sink fight — where everything from last Tuesday to three years ago gets thrown in — resolves nothing.

"Let's stay with this one thing right now. We can talk about the other stuff, but not in this same conversation."

**4. Say what you need, not what they're failing at.**

Criticism describes what's wrong with them. A need statement describes what's missing for you. The nervous system hears them very differently.

Criticism: "You're so emotionally unavailable."
Need: "I need more connection. I need to feel like you want to be close to me."

Criticism: "You never help around the house."
Need: "I need us to share the load more evenly. I'm burning out."

**5. Repair within 24 hours.**

Research consistently shows: it's not whether couples fight, it's whether they repair. The repair window matters. Too soon and you're still flooded. Too late and resentment calcifies.

Within 24 hours, come back: "I want to talk about what happened. Here's what I'm sorry for. Here's what I need. Here's what I heard from you."

**6. Touch, if welcome.**

Physical reconnection — a hand on the arm, sitting close, a hug — can do what words can't. The nervous system regulates through co-regulation. But only if touch is welcome. Ask: "Can I hold your hand?" Don't assume.

### The Committed Couple's Conflict Agreement

Consider creating explicit agreements about how you'll handle conflict. Not during a fight — between them.

Sample agreements:

- "We take breaks when one of us is flooded. No one chases."
- "We don't bring up other issues during an active disagreement."
- "We don't use contempt — no eye-rolling, name-calling, or mockery."
- "We repair within a day. Not perfectly. Just a return."
- "We don't involve other people in our fights without permission."
- "We never threaten to leave unless we mean it."
- "We're allowed to disagree and still be okay."

Writing these down — together — creates a shared nervous system anchor. When conflict spikes, both of you have something to return to.

### Field Note: The Couple Who Stopped Leaving the Room

They had a pattern: one would escalate, the other would walk out. The one who stayed felt abandoned. The one who left felt overwhelmed. Both felt alone.

They made an agreement: no one leaves the room during conflict. Instead, either person can call a pause. "I need a pause. I'll be back in fifteen minutes." The other agrees. No chasing.

It changed everything. Not the content of the fights — those continued. But the container held. Neither felt abandoned. Neither felt trapped. The fights got shorter because neither was managing the terror of the other leaving.

## Working It Out With Chosen Family and Spirit Family

### The Specific Challenge

Chosen family — and what many call spirit family — occupies a unique position. These are people you've chosen as deeply as (or more than) biological family, often bonded through shared healing, spiritual practice, or mutual transformation.

The depth of these bonds is their strength and their complication. When conflict arises in spirit family, it often feels like a betrayal at a soul level — because the relationship was formed at a soul level.

### What Makes Spirit Family Conflict Different

- **The bond carries spiritual significance.** Disagreements can feel like violations of a sacred contract.
- **Expectations are high.** "We're supposed to be more evolved than this" creates pressure to bypass conflict rather than work through it.
- **Healing timelines are personal.** You may have entered the relationship at the same place in your journey but grown at different rates or in different directions.
- **Spiritual bypassing is common.** Conflict gets reframed as "lessons" or "karma" rather than addressed directly. "Everything happens for a reason" becomes a way to avoid the conversation.
- **The community is often small.** Conflict with one spirit family member can ripple through the entire circle.

### How to Work It Out

**1. Bring it down to earth.**

Spirit family conflict must be addressed at the human level, not only the spiritual. "I know we both believe everything is connected, but right now I'm hurt by what happened at the gathering, and I need to talk about it as two humans, not two souls."

The spiritual lens can return later. But the human conversation needs to happen first.

**2. Resist the "higher purpose" bypass.**

When someone says, "This conflict is just a lesson we're both learning," check whether that framing is being used to avoid accountability. Lessons don't excuse impact.

"I'm sure there's something to learn here. And also, what you said hurt me, and I need that acknowledged before I can receive the lesson."

**3. Honor the bond without worshipping it.**

The relationship can be deeply meaningful and still have problems. Spiritual connection doesn't make someone incapable of harm. Treating the bond as sacred doesn't mean treating it as above question.

"I love what we've built. And I need us to be honest with each other, even when it's uncomfortable. That's what makes it real."

**4. Differentiate growth from divergence.**

Spirit family members often grow in different directions. One explores plant medicine, another turns toward contemplative prayer. One moves toward community, another toward solitude.

This isn't betrayal. It's differentiation.

"We don't have to be on the same path to be family. We just need to respect each other's path."

**5. Protect the circle.**

When conflict arises in a small spiritual community, the temptation is to triangulate — to talk to other members about the person you're in conflict with. This fractures the circle.

Commit to direct conversation. "I want to talk to you about this, not about you."

### A Practice: The Sacred Repair

For spirit family conflict, consider this adapted repair framework:

- **Ground together.** Before speaking, take three breaths together. Come into presence.
- **Speak from the heart, not the wound.** "My heart is heavy because..." rather than "You did this..."
- **Listen without formulating your response.** Receive their words fully before you construct yours.
- **Name what's at stake.** "This relationship means something to me. That's why I'm here doing this."
- **Release the outcome.** Work toward resolution, but accept that the relationship may need to change form. Changed form is not failed form.
- **Return to shared ground.** After the conversation, acknowledge what you still share — the values, the love, the vision — even if the specifics have shifted.

### Field Note: The Spirit Sisters Who Found Their Way Back

They called each other soul sisters. Five years of ceremonies, retreats, and shared healing.

Then one of them started questioning the teacher they both followed. The other felt betrayed — as if her sister was betraying something sacred, not just an opinion.

They didn't speak for four months.

When they finally sat together, the one who'd questioned the teacher said: "I didn't leave you. I left something that was no longer working for me. You are not the same as that."

The other wept. Not because she agreed. Because she finally heard: the bond was separate from the beliefs.

They rebuilt. Differently. Without the teacher. Without the ceremonies. Just two women who chose each other, even when their paths diverged.

## Working It Out With Biological Family You've Chosen to Keep

### The Specific Challenge

Biological family you've chosen to maintain — not out of obligation, but because you genuinely want them in your life — carries its own complexity.

These relationships come pre-loaded. You didn't choose the initial terms. The patterns were installed before you had language. The roles were assigned before you had a voice. And yet, something in the relationship has enough value that you're choosing to stay.

This is different from the family relationships addressed in Chapter 16 (Sovereignty With Family of Origin), which focuses on holding boundaries in systems you may not have chosen. This section is about the family members you've looked at clearly and said: "Yes. I want you in my life. Now how do we make this work?"

### What Makes Family Conflict Different

- **History is ever-present.** You're never just arguing about the thing in front of you. You're arguing about thirty years of things.
- **Roles are deeply encoded.** The peacekeeper, the scapegoat, the golden child, the fixer — these roles don't vanish because you've done inner work. They resurface under stress.
- **The nervous system responds differently.** Family activates the earliest attachment programming. You may find yourself regressing — acting younger, feeling smaller — in ways that don't happen in any other relationship.
- **Other family members are affected.** Working it out with one family member often creates ripples through the system. Other members may resist the change, take sides, or attempt to restore the old dynamic.

### How to Work It Out

**1. Separate the person from the role.**

Your mother is not just "your mother." She's a person with her own wounds, limitations, and history. When you can see her as a whole person — not just the role she occupied in your childhood — conflict becomes more navigable.

"I'm talking to you right now, not to the mother-role from my childhood. I need us to meet each other where we actually are."

**2. Expect the regression — and ground through it.**

When your father raises his voice and you suddenly feel eight years old, that's normal. The nervous system is doing what it was trained to do. Ground yourself physically — feet on the floor, breath slowed — and remind yourself: "I'm an adult. This is not then."

**3. Don't require them to understand your framework.**

You may have the language of attachment, sovereignty, nervous system regulation. They may not. Working it out doesn't require them to adopt your vocabulary. It requires you to translate your needs into language they can hear.

Instead of: "You're activating my attachment system."
Try: "When you criticize how I parent, I shut down. I need you to trust me."

**4. Set the boundary and stay in relationship.**

This is the hardest part. Boundaries and connection coexisting.

"I love you, and I'm not going to discuss my marriage with you. We can talk about a hundred other things. That one is off the table."

The boundary protects the relationship, not despite it. Without the boundary, resentment would eventually end the relationship anyway.

**5. Accept the ceiling.**

Some family relationships will only go so far. The depth you want may not be available. Working it out sometimes means accepting the version of the relationship that's actually possible — not the fantasy version you wish existed.

"My father can't do emotional depth. He can do fishing trips and fixing my car. That's his language for love. I can accept that without pretending it's everything I need."

### Field Note: The Brother Who Came Back

They hadn't really talked — genuinely talked — in twelve years.

Small talk at holidays. Texts about logistics. A careful dance around everything that mattered.

He finally said it: "I want a real relationship with you. Not the one we've been performing. But I need you to stop treating me like I'm still the little brother who doesn't know anything."

His brother was quiet. Then: "I didn't know I was doing that."

"You've been doing it since I was fifteen."

It took months. Several uncomfortable conversations. A few texts that went unanswered for days.

But they found something. Not the brotherhood of a movie. Something quieter. More honest. Two men who share blood and history, choosing to actually know each other.

## The Universal Framework: Five Practices for Working It Out

Whatever the relationship type, these five practices apply.

### 1. Show Up Honest, Not Right

Working it out requires honesty, not victory. The goal is not to win the argument. The goal is to be known — and to know them.

"I'm not trying to be right. I'm trying to be understood. And I want to understand you."

When both people are trying to be right, no one can be reached. When both people are trying to be honest, resolution becomes possible.

### 2. Tolerate the Discomfort of Unresolution

Not everything resolves in one conversation. Some things take weeks. Some take years. Some never fully resolve — you just learn to hold them.

The ability to sit with unresolved tension, without forcing premature closure, is one of the most important skills in committed relationships.

"We don't have to solve this tonight. We just have to stay in it together."

### 3. Track Your Nervous System, Not Just Your Thoughts

Your body is giving you information your mind may be overriding.

If your chest is tight, you're activated. If your jaw is clenched, you're bracing. If you've stopped breathing fully, you're in protection mode.

Before you say the next thing, notice what your body is doing. Regulate first. The conversation can wait for your nervous system to catch up.

### 4. Repair the Repair

Sometimes repair attempts go badly. You try to reconcile and it makes things worse. The conversation escalates when it was supposed to de-escalate.

When this happens, repair the repair.

"I came to that conversation wanting to fix things, but I think I made it worse. I'm sorry. Can we try again?"

There is no limit to how many times you can return. The willingness to keep coming back is the commitment.

### 5. Celebrate the Staying

Working it out is unglamorous. No one throws a party for the fight you survived. No one awards medals for the conversation you had at midnight about the thing you've been avoiding.

But these moments are where relationships are actually built. Not in the easy times. In the returns.

Acknowledge it — to yourself and to them.

"I know that was hard. I'm glad we did it. I'm glad we're still here."

## When Working It Out Isn't Working

Sometimes you try everything and it doesn't land.

You show up honest. They stonewall. You name the issue. They deflect. You regulate yourself. They escalate. You keep coming back. They keep leaving.

**Working it out requires two people.**

If you are the only one committed to the work, you're not in a repair process. You're in a performance of one.

**Signs that working it out may not be possible:**

- You've named the issue clearly, multiple times, and nothing changes
- Repair attempts are met with contempt, blame, or silence
- You leave every conversation feeling worse, not closer
- The other person refuses to take any responsibility
- Your sovereignty is consistently undermined when you try to address problems
- You're doing all the emotional labor while they watch

**This is not failure.** This is information.

Commitment doesn't mean enduring indefinitely. It means showing up fully, honestly, and consistently — and if that's not reciprocated, honoring what you've learned.

Sometimes the most sovereign thing you can do is say: "I tried. I really tried. And this isn't working."

That's not abandoning the relationship. That's honoring it enough to tell the truth.

## The Ongoing Practice

Working it out is not a skill you master once. It's a practice you return to, across relationships, across years, across the evolving versions of yourself and the people you love.

Every relationship will ask you to do it again — differently, with a new person, at a new level.

**What deepens over time:**

- Your ability to stay present during activation
- Your willingness to hear things that are hard
- Your capacity to hold both love and disappointment simultaneously
- Your skill at translating internal experience into words
- Your trust that the relationship can survive the truth

**What remains constant:**

- The choice to show up
- The willingness to be uncomfortable
- The commitment to honesty over harmony
- The practice of returning — to yourself and to them
