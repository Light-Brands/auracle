# Sage: Sensitivity Review — Vol 2 (Cultural-Coverage Pass)

I read the seven highest-density cultural-coverage passages in Vol 2 in the form they currently exist: deliberately structural, author-voice pattern-naming from outside the communities the author does not hold first-person authority in. I did not read whole chapters — only the cultural-coverage sections and their immediate context. My job was to notice tropes, reductive or othering framings, accuracy of patterns, power dynamics, and moments where a reader from the named community might pause.

This review is supplemental. It does not substitute for readers from Black, Asian (E/S/SE), Indigenous, Middle Eastern/SWANA, Evangelical/LDS/JW/Catholic, or trans communities. The author already has a plan to bring in community readers for the first-person voice drafting; these notes are meant to help the structural scaffolding be as honest and non-harming as possible *before* those readers arrive, so community readers can spend their energy on voice and nuance rather than cleaning up structural choices. Read with me as one voice, not the voice.

## Overall Observations

1. **The author's repeated framing sentence — "The structural pattern above holds across communities. The community-internal voice for each is being written by people who hold it" — is doing real ethical work.** It's the right move and I'd keep it. It signals the author knows where their authority ends. My one note is that this caveat appears almost identically in at least three places; a reader moving front-to-back will feel the repetition. Consider varying the phrasing or centralizing the commitment somewhere early (introduction or a front-matter "note on scope") so each instance can be shorter.

2. **The cultural armors are named in pattern-language ("patriarchal authority by role," "the strong mother who must not break") but the examples drop quickly into named communities (Black fathers, Korean restraint, Marianismo, Tiger Mother, Indigenous elders).** The risk of pattern-first / example-second framing is that the named community becomes the exemplar of the pattern — which can read as "this is where that pattern *lives*." A reader from those communities may feel typecast even when the sentence is accurate. I've flagged the sharpest instances per passage.

3. **"Tiger Mother" and "Machismo" are used as shorthands without air-quotes or attribution.** They are real terms but they're also contested inside their own communities (Tiger Mother is Amy Chua's frame; many Asian-American women have pushed back on it). Using them as if they're neutral diagnostic categories imports their weight. Worth a sentence somewhere marking them as in-community coinages that are themselves debated.

4. **The frame "naming harm vs. protecting the community from outside distortion" (Ch 5, Black fathers passage; Ch 12, community-as-relationship) is handled with unusual care and I want to name that.** The double-bind is real, and the author holds both sides rather than collapsing one into the other. This is the cultural-coverage writing at its best and worth using as the template for the sections still to be drafted.

5. **Religious-tradition language is the least even across these passages.** "The father coded as God" (Ch 5) and "Marian suffering-as-holiness" (Ch 6) work as structural descriptions, but the Appendix C per-tradition resource list (LDS, JW, Evangelical, Catholic) is where specific framings could land wrong for insiders — I've flagged those.

## Per-Passage Notes

### Ch 5: "When the Father's Authority Is Culturally Armored"
**File:** `/home/user/auracle/book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/05-father-archetype.md` (lines 400–416)

**What I noticed:**
- The six bullets move from generic ("patriarchal authority by role") to very specific (Black fathers, Indigenous elders, deportation/incarceration). The specific bullets are the ones where a community reader will have the strongest reaction — partly because they're accurate, partly because they're the only places the pattern lands on a named group.
- The Black-fathers bullet (line 409) does something important: it holds both "naming harm inside the home" and "the world's distorting lens" simultaneously. The sentence "Silence becomes protection of the people *and* protection of the harm" is a clean articulation of a bind many Black readers have written about. I'd keep this almost as-is.
- The Indigenous-elder bullet (line 408) — "an elder father may be one of a small number of people holding the language, the ceremony, or the memory" — is true and important. My hesitation: the bullet frames this as a *pressure on the child*, which it is, but the phrasing risks reading as "Indigenous cultural survival makes abuse harder to name" without also noting that Indigenous communities have their own internal accountability traditions, some of which predate and survived colonization. The current framing centers the bind without the repair.
- "Machismo names one version. Religious headship names another. Clan-patriarch authority names a third." (line 406) — this sentence flattens three very different traditions into interchangeable examples of the same mechanism. They *do* share the mechanism, but a Latino reader, a conservative Protestant reader, and an Arab/SWANA reader would each probably want their version named with slightly more specificity — or the list shortened to one example with "and others" so it doesn't read as a taxonomy.
- "The father coded as God" (line 411) is structurally correct for Evangelical/LDS/headship traditions but worth double-checking whether "cosmological" is the word the author wants. For readers still inside those traditions, "cosmological" may read as anthropological-outside-observer language.

**Potential impact:** Readers from the named communities may feel the sections about them are accurate but compressed — correct sentences that nonetheless do a lot of work per word. Readers *not* from those communities may take the bullets as complete rather than as gestures toward fuller treatment.

**Considerations:**
- Consider a short lead-in sentence naming that these bullets are *pointers*, not *summaries*, of the fuller per-community work.
- The Indigenous bullet would land more honestly with one more clause acknowledging internal accountability traditions, even briefly.
- Consider whether "machismo / religious headship / clan-patriarch" needs all three or would land better as one named example plus "and others."

**Priority:** MEDIUM — the structural honesty is already doing most of the work; these are refinements rather than corrections.

### Ch 6: "When the Mother's Suffering Is Culturally Sanctified"
**File:** `/home/user/auracle/book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/06-mother-archetype.md` (lines 515–530)

**What I noticed:**
- The framing sentence at line 528 — "Many readers discover, in recovery, that their mother was also harmed — by the same system that harmed them, often by the same man. Holding both at once is the work." — is the ethical core of the whole passage and it lands. I'd protect this sentence.
- "The strong mother who must not break" (line 522) is almost certainly pointing at the Strong Black Woman schema (and the closing list at line 530 confirms this). The bullet holds the pattern without naming the community, which reads more respectfully in my ear than if "Strong Black Woman" were used as a label mid-bullet. However, line 530 then lists "Strong Black Woman" as one of the per-community treatments to come. Worth checking whether the author wants to preview the term in the structural bullet or leave it un-named until the community-voice drafting. Either choice is defensible; consistency matters.
- "The achievement mother" (line 523) reads as the Tiger Mother / Asian-immigrant pattern without naming it. Same choice-point as above. My preference (as Sage, one voice) is that leaving it un-named in the structural bullet and naming it only in the "to be drafted" closing list is the more careful move — it describes the pattern as a pattern, not as Asian.
- "The restrained mother as pre-gaslighter" with the phrase "Korean emotional-restraint as pre-gaslighting" in the closing paragraph (line 530) — I'd flag this as the item most likely to cause a Korean or Korean-diaspora reader to pause. "Pre-gaslighter" is a strong coinage. It may be accurate to the author's experience or to readers they've worked with, but applied to an entire cultural affect (*han*, jeong, emotional restraint as family practice) it can read as pathologizing a cultural grammar rather than naming a harm that sometimes travels through it. Consider whether "pre-gaslighting climate" or "climate that pre-installed the doubt a later gaslighter exploited" might land less absolutely. I'd flag this one for a Korean reader specifically.
- "Marianismo" is named in the closing list (line 530) without being named in the structural bullet (line 521). The structural bullet — "self-sacrificing mother as moral ideal" — is gentler and does the work without the label. I'd keep it that way.
- "Patriarchal-clan protection of sons over daughters" (line 530) applies across many cultures (South Asian, SWANA, Orthodox Jewish, some Latin American, some East Asian). The bullet at line 525 is good; the concern is that by naming it "patriarchal-clan" in the closing list, SWANA and South Asian readers may feel they're being specifically tagged when the pattern is broader. Consider whether "clan" is the word — in SWANA scholarship "clan" has specific meanings that may not travel the way the author intends.

**Potential impact:** Overall this passage is stronger than Ch 5's equivalent because the *mechanism* is described before the community is named. The closing list at line 530 is where the risk concentrates — it's where specific communities get tagged with specific pathologies, even with the caveat that community voice will handle them.

**Considerations:**
- "Korean emotional-restraint as pre-gaslighting" is the single phrase I would most want a Korean-American reader to see before this goes to print.
- Consider whether the closing list's function (preview of what's coming) could be accomplished without pairing community + diagnosis in each entry. E.g., "Marianismo, Strong Black Woman, Tiger Mother, and others" — leaving the specific framing of each to the sections themselves.
- The "she was also harmed" line (528) is strong enough to carry more weight — consider giving it its own paragraph rather than living at the end of the bullet list.

**Priority:** HIGH — specifically the "Korean emotional-restraint as pre-gaslighting" phrase and the closing community+diagnosis pairing.

### Ch 11: "When the Body Was Pre-Trained to Override Itself"
**File:** `/home/user/auracle/book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/11-when-body-remembers.md` (lines 174–195)

**What I noticed:**

- This passage uses more in-language terms than any other — *aguanta*, *chī kǔ* (吃苦), *sabr*, *fuerte* / *la mujer fuerte*. I want to name first that the *choice* to do this is the right one. Using the internal vocabulary honors the communities whose virtues are being named. I'd keep that architecture. The risk is in how each word is handled.

- *Sabr* (line 184): "In its original spiritual form it is a profound discipline. Weaponized inside an abusive marriage, it becomes a life sentence." This is the single best handling in the bullet set — it preserves the sacred meaning of the term before naming what abuse does to it. A Muslim reader should recognize their tradition rather than feel it has been diagnosed. Keep.

- *Chī kǔ* (line 183): "Endurance as virtue. The body is taught that complaint is weakness and that quiet suffering is the highest moral achievement." Accurate to the pattern and compressed. The sentence "quiet suffering is the highest moral achievement" reads slightly more accusatory than the *sabr* sentence — it names the virtue as the harm rather than naming what happens when the virtue is weaponized. Consider mirroring the *sabr* structure: name the original meaning, then name what abuse does inside it.

- *Aguanta* (line 182) and *fuerte / la mujer fuerte* (line 186) are two bullets on Spanish-speaking patterns, placed apart. For a Latina reader reading front-to-back, the two bullets together can read as "your culture trained you to override yourself twice." Both are accurate. Consider whether they belong as one richer bullet rather than two, or whether one footnote acknowledging that both carry the same underlying grammar would let the list breathe.

- "Be strong / don't cry / keep it moving — the Anglophone Black and working-class inheritance of unbreakability" (line 185): compressing Black and working-class into one bullet is efficient but the two traditions have different histories. Black unbreakability specifically is rooted in chattel slavery and its aftermath — a specificity that a Black reader may want named rather than folded into a cross-class bullet. Consider whether this is one bullet or two.

- "The model-minority body" (line 189): "trained to succeed without complaint, where visible struggle is interpreted by the outside world as failure of the community, not of the conditions." Good framing — it names the external pressure (how the outside world reads struggle) rather than locating the problem inside Asian bodies. Keep.

- "Don't air dirty laundry" (line 187) as a near-universal: the sentence "across nearly every community" is true and is also a place where a reader may feel their specific version — *qi bu de* in Chinese, the specific weight of *sharam* in South Asian households, the Black church "what happens in this house" — is being universalized into a generic phrase. Consider whether this bullet is the one where the in-language register is most conspicuously absent.

- The closing sentence of the section (line 193): "doing that without betraying the ancestors whose endurance was a form of love." This is one of the strongest sentences in the chapter. It holds the ancestor as loved rather than as the source of harm. Keep and protect.

**Potential impact:** The passage is doing some of the most delicate work in the book — using community vocabulary to name community adaptations. A community reader will mostly feel honored by the attempt and will catch individual word-level choices where the register wobbles.

**Considerations:**
- Mirror the *sabr* structure (original meaning, then what abuse does to it) in the *chī kǔ* bullet.
- Consider whether *aguanta* and *fuerte* want to be one bullet.
- Consider whether Black unbreakability wants its own bullet rather than being paired with Anglophone working-class.
- The "dirty laundry" bullet is the one place where in-language phrasing would strengthen the passage.

**Priority:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The architecture is strong; the register calibration is where a community reader will earn their keep.

### Ch 12: "When the Community Is the Relationship"
**File:** `/home/user/auracle/book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/12-minimal-contact.md` (lines 215–250)

**What I noticed:**

- This is the passage I want to most directly commend. Line 230 — "often are not malicious. Many of them love you. They are doing what their culture taught them love looks like" — is the sentence that makes the whole section work. It prevents the passage from becoming an exit manifesto and holds the community as fellow-humans doing what they were taught. Keep.

- Line 221 lists the contexts: "collectivist culture, a faith community, an immigrant family, a small queer or trans community, an Indigenous nation." I paused on "Indigenous nation" in this list. Nation is the correct word (not "tribe," not "culture") and is appropriate to Indigenous political and legal status. Good. I'd watch one thing: "leaving the nation" has a different valence than "leaving a congregation" — an Indigenous person choosing distance from a specific family or band is not the same as disaffiliating from a nation, which may not be possible or desirable. The current phrasing handles this implicitly; a community reader may want it handled explicitly.

- Line 227 ("Losing the tribal community where leaving means becoming, in some sense, no one your ancestors would recognize") uses "tribal community" where line 221 used "Indigenous nation." The shift from nation to tribal is worth checking. "Tribal" is accepted by many Indigenous communities and contested by others; "nation" is more consistently respectful in U.S./Canadian contexts. Consider consistent vocabulary.

- Line 224 ("the congregation, the ward, the kingdom hall, the parish") names LDS ("ward"), JW ("kingdom hall"), and Catholic ("parish") specifically, plus the generic "congregation." This is careful and I'd keep it. One note: the list could also include "the mosque, the temple, the sangha" for non-Christian religious exits — but the specific traditions named (LDS, JW, Catholic, generic Evangelical congregation) are the ones where institutional exit is hardest and most literature-covered. If the author's intent is to name the hardest-exit traditions specifically, the current list is correct. If the intent is to be comprehensive, it's incomplete.

- Line 226 ("the chosen-family network that replaced the biological family that rejected you for being queer or trans") is doing good work. One small note: "replaced" is strong. Many queer/trans people hold chosen family *alongside* biological family rather than as replacement. "The chosen-family network that became family after the biological family rejected you" might hold the same weight with less either/or.

- Line 240 ("Find at least one ally inside the community") is excellent. The framing — "sit with you and say *both this is real and you are still one of us*" — is exactly what a survivor from a tight-knit community most needs to hear is possible.

- Line 246 ("Distinguish leaving the person from leaving the cosmology... You can leave a marriage without leaving God. You can leave a congregation without losing your soul. You can leave a tradition's institutional form while keeping its prayer, its language, its ancestors.") is the strongest theological sentence in the chapter for readers exiting religious traditions. It would land especially well for LDS, JW, Evangelical, and Catholic readers who often get told by their tradition that leaving the institution *is* apostasy. Keep.

- One structural watch: the list of what is lost (lines 223–228) is six items; the list of how to do the work (lines 236–246) is six items. That parallel is satisfying to read. I noticed that the *losses* list is more specific (language, food, ward, kingdom hall) than the *work* list (which is more universal). That asymmetry is fine and probably intentional — the losses are community-specific, the work is transferable.

**Potential impact:** A reader from a collectivist community is likely to feel this passage more accurately maps their reality than anything else in the recovery literature. The risk is low. The specific word-level places to watch are "tribal" vs. "nation" consistency and "replaced" in the chosen-family line.

**Considerations:**
- Consistent terminology: Indigenous nation vs. tribal community.
- "Replaced" → "became family after" in the chosen-family line.
- If comprehensive religious coverage is the intent, consider adding mosque/temple/sangha to the list of places; if hardest-exit-traditions is the intent, keep as-is.

**Priority:** LOW-MEDIUM. This passage is close to ready.

### Ch 13: expanded "Cultural and Religious Guilt"
**File:** `/home/user/auracle/book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/13-guilt-after-distance.md` (lines 234–291)

**What I noticed:**

- The generic section (lines 234–272) is sturdy and traditional; the risk concentrates in the expanded "How Guilt Is Architected Across Communities" section (lines 274–291), which pairs each community with a short architectural sketch. This is the densest community-tagging in the book and the place a reader from any of the named communities is most likely to feel catalogued.
- **Hispanic / Latino** (line 278): packs familismo, respeto, marianismo, machismo, and vergüenza into a single bullet. Each of those is its own scholarly literature. The in-language sentence *después de todo lo que esta familia ha hecho por ti* is well-chosen and lands. The "often thirty or more people" detail is vivid and accurate to some families but may read as a universalization of extended-family size. Consider softening to "the extended family, which in many households is large enough that calls begin within hours."
- **Black / African American** (line 279): "The Black church as community infrastructure where leaving the marriage means leaving the congregation" is a true and underwritten observation. The closing sentence — "The guilt is not just personal; it is racial, historical, and communal" — is good. My hesitation: the bullet stacks Strong Black Woman + Strong Black Man + church + "don't air dirty laundry" + *our people have been through worse* into five distinct mechanisms in two sentences. This is the bullet where a Black reader will most likely want the density unpacked, or want the author to pick two mechanisms and let those breathe.
- **Asian (East, South, Southeast)** (line 280): folds three enormous regional traditions into a single bullet. "East, South, Southeast" spans Korea, China, Japan, India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Thailand, the Philippines, Indonesia, and more — each with distinct family grammars. The bullet's content (filial piety, honor/shame, model minority, emotional restraint) mostly indexes East Asian experience; South Asian readers may feel their specific architecture (joint family, arranged-marriage dynamics, caste, *izzat* / *sharam*) is absent. Consider whether this wants to be two bullets (East Asian + South/Southeast Asian) or whether the current bullet wants language naming that it is a pointer, not a summary.
- **Indigenous / Native** (line 281): "Tribal belonging as inseparable from identity" — "tribal" again, where line 221 of Ch 12 used "nation." Same terminology-consistency note as in my Ch 12 comments. "The culture is endangered and every member matters to its survival" is accurate and heavy; worth checking with an Indigenous reader whether this framing, even when true, puts additional weight on the individual survivor rather than on the colonial conditions that created the endangerment.
- **Middle Eastern / Arab / SWANA** (line 282): "Sharaf and ird" are correctly distinguished (they are not synonyms; ird is specifically women's sexual honor, sharaf is broader family honor). This is careful work. "Patriarchal authority that owes no justification" is a strong claim about a very diverse region (SWANA includes Lebanese Christians, Iranian Baháʼís, Kurdish families, Egyptian Copts, Moroccan Jews, secular urban Arab families, etc.). Consider whether "in many traditionalist households" or similar would hold the specificity without flattening the region.
- **Religious traditions** (line 283): "Jehovah's Witness shunning, the most efficient coercive mechanism in modern Western religion" is a strong superlative. It may be accurate to the author's reading and to much exit literature, but stated as fact rather than as the author's view it may read as polemical to a current JW reader who might otherwise hear the rest of the chapter. Consider attributing it ("many exit scholars describe JW disfellowshipping as…") or softening to "among the most efficient."
- **Religious traditions** (line 283): "Catholic sacrament and Catholic guilt" — "Catholic guilt" is a culturally familiar shorthand but it's also the one phrase in the bullet set that uses a stereotype-coded label. Everywhere else the author names the mechanism (headship, eternal sealing, shunning). Consider naming the Catholic mechanism specifically — sacramental indissolubility of marriage, the confessional's internal forum, the communion of saints as ongoing obligation — rather than using the pop-culture phrase.
- **LGBTQ+ / Trans-specific** (lines 284–285): the "acceptance trap" framing is good and rarely articulated this clearly. "Medical, social, documentary, and existential at once" is a precise compression of the trans-specific bind. Keep. One note: these two bullets are separated, which is correct (trans-specific is not a subset of LGBTQ+ experience), but the trans bullet is shorter than the LGB bullet. Consider whether that asymmetry is what the author wants.
- **Immigrant families** (line 286): "We crossed an ocean / a desert / a war" is a strong line. Worth checking whether "a war" risks sounding glib next to "an ocean." Most readers will read it as the escalating list the author intends; a few may hear it as collapsing very different migration experiences.
- **Male survivors** (line 287): placed last, after everyone else. The content is accurate. Placement worth considering — a male survivor reading this list may feel their category arrived as an afterthought. Alternatively, placing it last may be the author's deliberate de-centering, which is defensible.

**Potential impact:** This is the section where readers across almost every community will test whether the author has earned the right to name the pattern. The architecture sketches are mostly accurate; the risk is density (too many mechanisms per bullet) and labels (Catholic guilt, tribal, the JW superlative) rather than content.

**Considerations:**
- Consider whether Asian wants to be one bullet or two (East vs. South/Southeast).
- Consider unpacking the Black bullet — five mechanisms in two sentences is the densest in the list.
- "Catholic guilt" → name the specific sacramental mechanism instead.
- Attribute or soften the JW superlative.
- Terminology consistency: "tribal" vs. "nation."
- Soften "patriarchal authority that owes no justification" to name which SWANA households it holds in.

**Priority:** HIGH — this is the single highest-density community-tagging passage in the book and the one most likely to land wrong if individual word-level choices aren't calibrated. The architecture is right; the calibration is what a community reader will most affect.

### Ch 22: "Reparenting When Needs Themselves Were Taboo"
**File:** `book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/chapters/22-reparenting-practices.md` (lines 128–145)

**What I noticed:**

- The framing sentence "the work is the permission to have a need at all" is the thesis of the section and it lands. It does the structural work without prescribing what permission looks like inside any specific tradition. Keep.
- The opening paragraph (line 132) names that the inherited voices "are not always wrong. They kept a generation alive. And they still have to be overruled." This is the rare framing that honors the inheritance while refusing it; readers from collectivist and survival-coded communities are more likely to stay with the chapter because of this sentence specifically.
- **"Having needs was classed as selfish"** (line 136): the bullet groups Asian, Latin, SWANA, Indigenous, African, and immigrant family systems into one "collectivist" frame. This is structurally true and the bullet labors hard to be inclusive — but the move from "collectivist family systems" to a single reparent script (*you are allowed to want this*) treats six culturally distinct grammars as substitutable. Consider whether the reparent script wants a one-line acknowledgement that "what 'allowed' sounds like inside each frame differs — that translation is the work the per-community pass will do."
- **"Self-compassion was classed as weakness"** (line 137): pairs Black women's inheritance + *aguanta* + *chī kǔ* + *sabr* + Protestant stoicism in a single bullet. The compression is elegant but it places a Black woman's "Strong Black Woman" inheritance, a Mandarin-speaking child of Chinese parents, an Arabic-speaking SWANA child, and a white New England Protestant in the same conditioning category. The mechanisms differ. The lived weight differs. Consider whether the in-language terms want their own line each, or whether the bullet wants to name explicitly that these are *related but not equivalent* endurance codes.
- **"Receiving was classed as debt"** (line 138): cleanly observed and unlikely to misread. Strong bullet.
- **"Rest required permission that was never granted"** (line 139) and **"Modesty and invisibility were classed as virtue"** (line 140): both well-framed. The "for queer and trans children in rejecting families … *not being seen* was the skill that kept the family safe" line is one of the more careful trans-adjacent observations in the book — it names invisibility as survival skill rather than as deficit. Keep.
- **"Individual identity itself was classed as betrayal"** (line 141): the reparent script *"You can love the people and still have a self. The self is not the betrayal. The disappearance would be."* is good. It does what reparenting prose has to do — short, declarative, addressed to the body rather than the analytic mind. A reader from a clan-coded culture may want to test whether "self" itself is the right word, given that "self" carries individualist-frame baggage in some traditions; "soul," "interiority," "the part of you that knows" can all do the same work without importing the frame the chapter is trying to refuse.
- The closing paragraph (line 143) — "this does not mean the culture was wrong and the individualist frame is right. Most of these injunctions evolved for reasons — survival reasons, love reasons" — is the single most important sentence in the chapter for a reader from any of the six communities. Without this sentence, the section reads as Western therapy critiquing collectivism. With it, the section reads as an offer. Keep, and consider whether it wants to come earlier (currently it lands after the bullets; some readers will close the chapter before reaching it).
- The pointer to the per-community pass (line 145) is doing real work. It tells the reader the author knows this is a sketch, not a translation. Keep.

**Potential impact:** This is the most generous version of "naming patterns from outside" the chapter could write — it honors the inheritance, refuses the inheritance's full cost, and points to where the in-community translation will happen. The risk is in the bullet-level compression: each bullet bundles three to six culturally distinct experiences into a single reparent script, and a reader from any one of those communities will feel the bundle. The fix is mostly small — add one disclaimer line per bullet, or reorder so the closing "the culture was not wrong" paragraph leads.

**Considerations:**
- Consider moving the "the culture was not wrong" paragraph (line 143) earlier — perhaps before the bullets — so the frame is set before the reader meets the structural compressions.
- Consider whether the in-language terms in line 137 want their own bullet each, with one-line acknowledgement that the endurance codes are related but not equivalent.
- Consider the word "self" in line 141 — is there a frame-neutral alternative ("interiority," "the part of you that knows") that would land for a reader from a clan-coded culture without importing the individualism the chapter is trying to refuse?
- Confirm with each community reader, when recruited, whether the *reparent scripts* (the italicized one-liners) translate inside their tradition's grammar, or whether each community's pass needs to write its own scripts.

**Priority:** MEDIUM — the section is doing careful work and the framing is more generous than most reparenting literature. The remaining risk is calibration at the bullet level and the placement of the framing paragraph.

### Appendix C: "Community-Specific Resources"
**File:** `book/sovereignty-series/vol-2-heal/appendices/appendix-c-resources.md` (lines 68–144)

**What I noticed:**

- The framing sentence (line 70) — "Each list is partial — the companion website maintains a current, expanded directory" — is the right disclaimer. Resource lists in print age fast; the pointer to the website does the longevity work.
- **Ordering:** Black, Asian, Indigenous, SWANA, Hispanic, Religious-Exit, LGBTQ+, Trans, Jewish, Male. The intent looks alphabetic-ish-by-historic-marginalization rather than alphabetic; that's a defensible editorial choice but it reads as if the author chose an order. Consider whether the order communicates anything inadvertent — for instance, Jewish appearing second-to-last when the author's own primary authority is Jewish could read either as humility or as deprioritization, depending on the reader.
- **Black / African American Survivors** (lines 72–78): five organizations, well-curated. Therapy for Black Girls and BEAM are correctly identified as flagship. "The Confess Project" is noted for Black men's mental health — good cross-gender inclusion in a section that could otherwise default to women-only. Ujima is correctly named as the National Center on Violence Against Women in the Black Community. Consider adding **Sista Afya** and **Black Women's Blueprint** for breadth (both are well-established).
- **Asian (East, South, Southeast)** (lines 80–86): five entries cover a region of more than two billion people. The selection skews North American diasporic (Asian Mental Health Collective, SASMHA, Manavi, Asian Women's Shelter, Subtle Asian Mental Health). For a reader in the diaspora this is appropriate; for a reader in any homeland it will read as an entirely diaspora-framed list. Consider whether the print appendix wants a one-line acknowledgement that this is a diaspora-focused list and the website holds the international one. Also consider adding **API-GBV** (Asian Pacific Institute on Gender-Based Violence) and **Sakhi for South Asian Women**, both of which are flagship.
- **Indigenous / Native** (lines 88–94): StrongHearts is correctly named with phone number. NIWRC, We R Native, Native Wellness Institute, Mending the Sacred Hoop are all well-chosen. The list is US-only — no Canadian First Nations, Native Hawaiian, Alaska Native, Métis, or Inuit organizations.
