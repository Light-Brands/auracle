## Part I: Foundational Decisions

### 1.1 Regional Variant

**Decision: Pan-Hispanic neutral Spanish**

The adaptation targets the broadest possible readership across Latin America and Spain.
Where regional splits exist (e.g., *computadora* vs. *ordenador*), prefer the term with
widest recognition. Avoid colloquialisms tied to a single country. When no neutral term
exists, prefer Latin American usage (larger market) and footnote the peninsular
alternative on first occurrence.

### 1.2 Register & Mode of Address

**Decision: Tú (informal second person singular)**

The English original speaks directly, intimately, and urgently to the reader — "You are
not crazy. Let that land." The *tú* form preserves this intimacy. *Usted* would create
clinical distance that contradicts the book's voice. *Vos* is too regionally marked.

**Consequence:** All imperatives, possessives, and verb conjugations follow *tú* form
throughout.

### 1.3 Gendered Language

**Decision: Feminine default with inclusive framing**

The primary audience is women healing from narcissistic abuse. The English text uses "you"
(gender-neutral) but the content, examples, and voice center women's experience. In
Spanish:

- Use feminine forms for the implied reader (*"estás cansada," "fuiste condicionada"*)
- Use masculine forms when describing the manipulator in examples that follow the
  original's framing
- Open the book with a translator's note: *"Este texto utiliza el femenino como forma
  principal por reflejar la experiencia mayoritaria de sus lectoras. Toda persona que se
  reconozca en estas páginas es bienvenida."*

### 1.4 Handling English Acronyms

Several key acronyms (DARVO, JADE, FOG, FLEAS) are established in English-language abuse
recovery communities and have entered Spanish-language forums untranslated. Policy:

- **Retain the English acronym** as the primary term
- **Provide a Spanish expansion** in parentheses on first use
- **Do not force Spanish acronyms** — artificial acronyms feel contrived and reduce
  searchability for readers seeking community support online

Example: *DARVO (Negar, Atacar, Invertir Víctima y Agresor)*

### 1.5 Decoder Card Naming Convention

Decoder card names function as **branded labels** — they must be:

- Memorable and speakable aloud
- Recognizable when referenced later in the text
- Consistent in grammatical structure within each category
- Short enough to work on a physical card format

The Spanish names aim for **conceptual equivalence over literal translation**. Where the
English uses an idiomatic phrase, the Spanish finds its own idiom of equal impact.

### 2.2 The Arc Words (The Mantra)

The eight arc words form the spine of the series. Each is a volume title and together they
read as a sentence of transformation.

| Vol | English | Spanish | Rationale |
|-----|---------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | SEE | **DESPERTAR** | "Ver" is flat and ambiguous (*ve* = see/go). *Despertar* captures the awakening from the fog — the lived experience of Vol 1, not just the act of looking. |
| 2 | HEAL | **SANAR** | Direct, warm, widely understood. *Curar* is too clinical/medical. |
| 3 | STAND | **SOSTENER** | *Sostenerte* (reflexive) = to hold yourself up. More embodied than *pararte* (stand up) which is merely physical. |
| 4 | LIVE | **VIVIR** | Universal. No alternative needed. |
| 5 | GIVE | **ENTREGAR** | *Dar* is too generic. *Entregar* carries weight — to hand over, to deliver, to give fully. Fits the generational healing theme. |
| 6 | SERVE | **SERVIR** | Direct equivalent. Context makes clear this is purposeful service, not servitude. |
| 7 | THRIVE | **FLORECER** | *Prosperar* is too financial. *Florecer* = to bloom, to flourish. Beautiful and embodied. |
| 8 | BECOME | **SER** | *Convertirte* is too mechanical. *Ser* = to be, to exist as yourself. The simplest, deepest word. "You were never becoming someone new — you were always *being* who you already were." |

**The Spanish Mantra:**
```
DESPERTAR → SANAR → SOSTENER → VIVIR → ENTREGAR → SERVIR → FLORECER → SER
```

## Part XIII: Usage Rules for Translators

### 13.1 Consistency Is Non-Negotiable

Every term in this bible has ONE Spanish equivalent. Use it every time. No synonym
rotation for "variety." Readers are learning a vocabulary — inconsistency undermines
recognition and safety.

### 13.2 When the English Sounds Better, Resist

The temptation to retain English for "coolness" should be resisted except for the terms
explicitly listed in Part XII. Spanish is a rich language. Trust it.

### 13.3 Rhythm Over Literalism

The mantras (Part VII) are meant to be read aloud, whispered in moments of crisis,
written on bathroom mirrors. If a literal translation loses the rhythm, restructure the
Spanish to land with the same breath and beat. Flag significant departures for author
review.

### 13.4 Gendered Nouns for the Manipulator

When referring to the narcissist/manipulator in general terms, use masculine as default
(matching the book's predominant example framing). When a specific vignette features a
female manipulator, switch accordingly. Never use *"narcisista"* as exclusively male or
female — it applies to all genders. The article (*el/la narcisista*) should match the
specific context.

### 13.5 Flagging Cultural Gaps

If a fairy tale, cultural reference, or idiom does not land in Spanish, do not silently
replace it. Flag it in the margin for author review with a proposed alternative and
rationale.
