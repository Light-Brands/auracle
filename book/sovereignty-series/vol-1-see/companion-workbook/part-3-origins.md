## Section 6: The Role You Were Assigned

*Aligned with Chapter 7 — Family Roles & Triangulation*

---

You didn't choose your role. It was assigned — probably before you could talk, definitely before you could object. Narcissistic family systems need each member to play a specific part, and the casting isn't based on who you are. It's based on what the system needs you to be.

This section helps you identify the role you were given, the rules that came with it, and the cost of playing it for all those years.

---

### Exercise 6A: Which Role Were You Cast In?

Read through the four roles below. You may recognize yourself immediately in one, or you may see pieces of yourself in several. Many people shifted roles over time — the Golden Child who fell from grace and became the Scapegoat, the Lost Child who became the Mascot to survive. Mark everything that resonates. There are no wrong answers.

**The Golden Child**

The favored one. The extension of the narcissist's pride. You were praised — but the praise was for performance, not for being you.

Check any that apply:

- [ ] I was the "good one," the achiever, the one they bragged about
- [ ] I felt pressure to be perfect — not from myself, but from them
- [ ] Love felt conditional on my performance, grades, behavior, or compliance
- [ ] I saw what happened to my sibling when they displeased the parent, and I was terrified of that happening to me
- [ ] I didn't know who I actually was underneath who they needed me to be
- [ ] I felt guilty for being favored
- [ ] I was called "the easy child" or "no trouble at all"
- [ ] I learned to read what they wanted and become it before they had to ask

**The Scapegoat**

The one who carried the family's shadow. Blamed, criticized, othered — often because you were the one who saw the truth and couldn't stop naming it.

Check any that apply:

- [ ] I was the "problem child," the difficult one, the one who caused trouble
- [ ] I pointed out things that were wrong and was punished for it
- [ ] I was compared unfavorably to a sibling
- [ ] I was told I was too sensitive, too angry, too dramatic, too much
- [ ] I internalized the belief that something was fundamentally wrong with me
- [ ] I was the reason family gatherings "went badly"
- [ ] Other family members seemed relieved when the attention was on me instead of them
- [ ] I sometimes became what they said I was, because at least it was an identity

**The Lost Child**

The invisible one. You learned that the safest thing to be was nothing — no needs, no noise, no presence that could attract the wrong kind of attention.

Check any that apply:

- [ ] I spent most of my childhood alone — in my room, in my head, in a book
- [ ] I learned not to need anything from anyone
- [ ] I was described as "easy" or "low-maintenance" (but the truth was I'd given up asking)
- [ ] My accomplishments went unnoticed or uncommented on
- [ ] I could disappear for hours and no one checked
- [ ] I felt like a guest in my own family
- [ ] I developed a rich inner world because the outer one wasn't safe
- [ ] I struggle to take up space, even now

**The Mascot**

The entertainer. You learned that humor could defuse tension, redirect rage, and make yourself valuable to the system. Your job was to lighten the mood — at the cost of anyone ever taking your pain seriously.

Check any that apply:

- [ ] I used humor to manage the family's emotional temperature
- [ ] I became the funny one, the charming one, the one who kept things light
- [ ] When things got tense, I felt responsible for fixing the energy in the room
- [ ] People were surprised when I was sad or angry — it didn't match their image of me
- [ ] I learned that my pain made people uncomfortable, so I packaged it as a joke
- [ ] My role was to entertain, not to feel
- [ ] I struggle to be taken seriously when I'm hurting
- [ ] The real me hid behind the performance

## Section 7: The Sentence You Spent Your Childhood Earning

*Aligned with Chapter 8 — Parental Wounds*

---

There's a sentence you've been waiting to hear your whole life. Not a grand speech. Not a long letter. Just a simple sentence — the thing your parent never said that you kept trying to earn through performance, compliance, perfection, silence, or self-erasure.

For some of us, it's "I'm proud of you." For others, it's "I see you." For others, it's simply "You're enough." Or "I'm sorry." Or "It wasn't your fault."

This section asks you to find that sentence. And then to begin the work of giving it to yourself.

### Exercise 7B: Your Parent's Wound Archetype

Chapter 8 identifies multiple parental wound archetypes — for both mother wounds and father wounds. These aren't diagnoses — they're patterns. Your parent may fit one clearly, or they may be a combination. Some archetypes appear in both sections because they show up differently depending on the parent.

Read through each description and mark whatever resonates. You may find that your mother and father each carry a different wound type.

---

**The Engulfing Parent**

Their love was a flood. Your autonomy was their abandonment. "I need space" was met with tears, guilt, or rage.

- [ ] They needed to know everything — where I was, who I was with, what I was thinking
- [ ] My independence was treated as betrayal
- [ ] I felt suffocated but couldn't name why, because they "loved me so much"
- [ ] Space between us felt dangerous — to them, and therefore to me

**The Emotionally Absent Parent**

Their body showed up. Their attention didn't. You couldn't point to what they did wrong. They just weren't there.

- [ ] They went through the motions but their eyes were somewhere else
- [ ] I performed louder, harder, more — trying to get them to really see me
- [ ] I concluded that I wasn't interesting or important enough to hold someone's attention
- [ ] I learned to need nothing, because needing was just another way to be disappointed

**The Jealous / Competitive Parent**

Your shine was their threat. Your beauty, your success, your happiness — it activated something in them that looked like resentment.

- [ ] They couldn't celebrate me without comparing themselves
- [ ] I learned to dim myself to keep the peace
- [ ] Compliments made me contract instead of expand
- [ ] I felt guilty when good things happened to me

**The Martyr Parent**

Their suffering was their identity — and your debt. "After everything I've done for you" wasn't a sentence. It was a lifestyle.

- [ ] I carry guilt like a low-grade fever that never breaks
- [ ] Receiving anything — gifts, help, love — triggers an immediate calculation of what I owe
- [ ] Setting a boundary feels like committing a crime
- [ ] I learned that my existence requires repayment

**The Covert / Sweetness Parent**

They never raised their voice. They never said anything overtly cruel. But you left every interaction feeling worse than when you walked in.

- [ ] The harm was invisible — no bruise to point to
- [ ] I felt crazy for feeling bad when they were "so nice"
- [ ] Conversations that seemed pleasant left me foggy and drained
- [ ] I couldn't explain what was wrong because nothing looked wrong from the outside

**The Vicariously Living Parent**

They didn't have their own life — they had yours. Your appearance, your achievements, your choices existed to fulfill their unlived dreams.

- [ ] They chose my clothes, my activities, my friends, my path
- [ ] My accomplishments were narrated as theirs
- [ ] I couldn't answer "What do you want?" because my wants were never the point
- [ ] I felt like I was living someone else's life

**The Rage-Based Parent**

Anger was the atmosphere. Not occasional frustration — a constant charge in the air that everyone organized around. You learned to read the room before you could read a book.

- [ ] I could sense their mood before they walked through the door
- [ ] The household revolved around not setting them off
- [ ] I learned to be small, quiet, and pre-emptively compliant
- [ ] I flinch at raised voices, sudden movements, or unexpected changes in tone

**The Addicted / Unpredictable Parent**

You never knew which version of them you were getting. The substance changed them — or maybe revealed them. Either way, you learned that consistency was a fantasy.

- [ ] I became hypervigilant — scanning for which version of them was coming home
- [ ] Good days felt dangerous because I knew they wouldn't last
- [ ] I became the parent — managing their moods, covering for them, holding things together
- [ ] I learned that hope is what hurts the most

**The Authoritarian Parent**

Obedience was the only currency. Questions were defiance. Boundaries were disrespect. Their word was law — not because it was wise, but because it was theirs.

- [ ] My opinions didn't exist until they were approved
- [ ] Compliance was rewarded; independence was punished
- [ ] I struggle to trust my own decisions because I was never allowed to make any
- [ ] "Because I said so" was the only explanation I ever got

**The Achievement-Obsessed Parent**

Love was a scoreboard. What mattered was performance — grades, trophies, visible success. You weren't celebrated for who you were, but for what you produced.

- [ ] Rest felt like failure; slowing down triggered anxiety
- [ ] I was praised for accomplishments but invisible otherwise
- [ ] I still can't separate my worth from my productivity
- [ ] Second place was the same as last place

**The Enmeshed / "Best Friend" Parent**

They treated you as a peer, a confidant, a co-parent. The boundary between parent and child dissolved — and you carried adult weight before you had adult resources.

- [ ] They told me things no child should know — about their marriage, their finances, their pain
- [ ] I was their emotional support system before I had language for my own emotions
- [ ] Closeness felt like a trap disguised as trust
- [ ] I struggle to tell the difference between intimacy and enmeshment

**The Idealizing / Pedestaling Parent**

They didn't see you — they saw a projection. You were perfect, special, destined for greatness. The pressure of the pedestal was its own cage.

- [ ] I couldn't be ordinary without disappointing them
- [ ] Their pride in me felt like ownership, not celebration
- [ ] Failure wasn't just failure — it was personal betrayal
- [ ] I learned to perform their version of me instead of discovering my own

**The Financially Weaponizing Parent**

Money was never just money — it was control, leverage, and proof of who held the power. Generosity came with strings. Independence meant ingratitude.

- [ ] Financial help always came with expectations or reminders
- [ ] I was made to feel guilty about money — for needing it, spending it, or having my own
- [ ] Their financial support was a leash, not a gift
- [ ] I struggle to feel safe about money, even when I have enough

## Section 9: Siblings & the System

*Aligned with Chapter 10 — Breaking Free*

---

In healthy families, siblings are your first allies. Your first friends. The people who were there for the same childhood and can say, "Yes, that happened. I remember it too."

In narcissistic families, siblings are often turned into adversaries. The system pits you against each other — competing for scraps of love that were never enough for either of you. The tragedy is that you were both harmed. And you were set up to never be able to talk about it.

This section helps you map your sibling relationships, grieve the alliance that was stolen, and practice the boundaries you need for the relationships you have now.

If you're an only child, some of these exercises may still resonate — the roles of sibling can be filled by cousins, close family friends, or other children in the system. Adapt what's useful. Skip what isn't.
