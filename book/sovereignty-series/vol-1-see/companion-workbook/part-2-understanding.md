## Section 4: The Bond That Felt Like Love

*Aligned with Chapter 4 — Trauma Bonds*

---

Chapter 4 explains the neuroscience behind why you stayed, why you went back, and why leaving felt like withdrawal. This section helps you map those patterns in your own experience, so you can see the machinery that was operating beneath what felt like love.

A reminder before we start: understanding the biology of the bond does not mean the love wasn't real. It means the love was wired to a system that was hurting you. Both things can be true.

---

### Exercise 4A: Slot Machine vs. Vending Machine

Chapter 4 introduces a core concept: intermittent, unpredictable rewards create stronger bonds than consistent, reliable ones. A vending machine gives you what you paid for every time. A slot machine might pay off, and that uncertainty is what makes it addictive.

In a healthy relationship, kindness is a vending machine. You put in connection, you get connection back. Predictably. Boringly. Beautifully.

In a trauma bond, kindness is a slot machine. You never know when it will pay out, so you keep pulling the lever. And when it does pay out, the relief is so intense it feels like proof that this is real.

**Part A: Map the unpredictability.**

Think about the person who comes to mind as you read this book. In the left column, list specific examples of kindness, warmth, or connection. In the right column, list what happened before or after each one. Look for the pattern: was the kindness predictable, or was it always a surprise that followed something painful?

### Exercise 4D: The 72-Hour Test Log

Chapter 4 discusses how love-bombing and the relief cycle flood your brain with neurochemicals that distort decision-making. The 72-Hour Test is simple: before making any major decision about the relationship, wait 72 hours. That's how long it takes for the neurochemistry to settle enough that you can think clearly.

This is not a willpower exercise. It's a biology exercise. Your brain needs time to come down from the dopamine spike so you can see the situation with your own eyes instead of through the haze of relief or craving.

**How to use this template:**

Each time you feel a strong urge to make a decision about the relationship (going back, calling them, forgiving without repair, agreeing to something that doesn't feel right), fill out one row. Wait 72 hours. Then fill out the last two columns.

### Exercise 5B: Injury Mapping

Chapter 5 explains narcissistic injury: the emotional devastation that occurs when the gap between their idealized self-image and reality becomes visible. The rage that follows is disproportionate because it's not really about what you said or did. It's about the threat to their entire self-concept.

This exercise asks you to map the "small" things that triggered disproportionate reactions. These moments are some of the most confusing in the relationship because the response never matched the stimulus. You said something minor. They reacted like you'd attacked their core.
