# Appendix D: Safety Planning Worksheet

> For readers in YELLOW or RED safety levels.
> If you are in immediate danger, call 911 or your local emergency number.
> If you are not in immediate danger but are unsafe, this worksheet helps you prepare.

---

## Before You Begin

This worksheet is for your eyes only. **Do not leave it where it can be found.** Consider:
- Storing it digitally with a password
- Keeping it at a trusted friend's house
- Using a private/incognito browser if accessing online
- Photographing and storing in a locked phone folder

If you cannot safely complete this worksheet, skip it. Your safety is more important than any exercise.

---

## Part 1: Assess Your Current Safety Level

Read each statement. Check the ones that apply to you right now.

### GREEN Indicators
- [ ] I am no longer in regular contact with this person
- [ ] I have a safe place to live that they do not control
- [ ] I have financial resources they cannot access
- [ ] I have a support network (friends, family, therapist)
- [ ] I feel physically safe

### YELLOW Indicators
- [ ] I am still in contact but there is no history of physical violence
- [ ] I have some support but it's limited
- [ ] I have an exit plan but haven't activated it
- [ ] I feel emotionally unsafe but not physically threatened
- [ ] I can access resources without being monitored

### RED Indicators
- [ ] There is a history of physical intimidation, threats, or violence
- [ ] I am being monitored (phone, computer, location)
- [ ] I am financially dependent with no access to funds
- [ ] I have been threatened with harm to me, my children, or my pets
- [ ] I feel unsafe leaving or setting boundaries
- [ ] Weapons are present in the home
- [ ] The violence has escalated recently
- [ ] They have threatened suicide if I leave

**If you checked ANY RED indicators, please contact:**
- **National Domestic Violence Hotline:** 1-800-799-7233
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text HOME to 741741
- A safety plan created with a professional is more effective than one created alone.

### Things I should NOT do when leaving:

- Do not go to the first place they would look for you
- Do not post on social media about your plans
- Do not answer calls from unknown numbers immediately after leaving
- Do not return alone to get belongings

### After I leave:
- Contact the domestic violence hotline for next steps
- File for a protective order if appropriate
- Change locks if you stay in the shared home
- Notify children's school about custody/pickup permissions
- Inform your employer if there is a safety concern at work

## Part 5: Safety While Staying

If leaving is not possible right now, these strategies can help:

### Emotional Safety
- Identify one person who knows the truth about your situation
- Use grounding techniques daily (Appendix C)
- Keep a hidden journal (digital, password-protected)
- Maintain at least one connection outside the relationship
- Practice gray rock in daily interactions to reduce conflict

### Physical Safety
- Know the exits in every room
- Avoid rooms where you could be cornered (bathrooms, closets)
- Keep your phone charged and accessible
- Have a bag ready (even a small one) at a friend's house or in your car
- If an argument is escalating, move toward an exit

### Financial Safety (when possible)
- Open a separate bank account they don't know about
- Save small amounts of cash in a safe location
- Document shared assets and debts
- Know your financial rights (many domestic violence organizations offer financial counseling)

## Part 6: For Specific Situations

### If They Threaten Suicide

This is a manipulation tactic described in the book (Card 21: Emotional Hostage-Taking). It is also potentially real. Both can be true.

**What to do:**
- Do not stay because of the threat
- Call 988 (Suicide Prevention Lifeline) or 911 on their behalf
- Contact their family member or friend to alert them
- You are not a crisis counselor. Professional help is appropriate.
- Their mental health is not your responsibility to manage through self-sacrifice

### If Children Are Involved

- Document concerning behavior (dates, times, specifics)
- Consult with a family law attorney before making custody-related decisions
- National Child Abuse Hotline: 1-800-422-4453
- Children's exposure to emotional abuse is recognized as harmful in most jurisdictions
- Many domestic violence organizations have children's advocates

### If You Share Pets

- If possible, rehome pets to a safe friend temporarily
- Some shelters accept pets or can connect you with foster care
- Document any threats made toward animals (this is a significant risk factor)
