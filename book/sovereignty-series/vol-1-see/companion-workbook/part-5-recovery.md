# Part V: Recovery, Response, and Moving Forward

### Companion Workbook Sections 14-16

### Aligned with Chapters 15-17

---

> You've done the hardest part. You saw it. You named it. You sat with the discomfort
> of knowing what you know. Everything from here is about building — not from scratch,
> but from truth.

---

If Parts I through IV asked you to look backward and inward, Part V asks you to look
forward. Not in a "positive vibes only" way — there's grief ahead, and complexity, and
days when you'll wonder if you're making progress at all. But the direction has changed.
You're no longer trying to figure out what happened. You're learning how to live now
that you know.

The exercises in this section are designed to be returned to. Some of them you'll do
once and feel complete. Others you'll revisit monthly, or whenever the old loops
activate. There's no expiration date on any of this. Use what serves you. Leave the
rest.

---

## Section 14: Energetic Remapping Journal

*Aligned with Chapter 15 — Recovery Tools*

---

This section works directly with the Four-Step Remapping Process from Chapter 15:

1. **Identify the loop**
2. **Ground physically**
3. **Reclaim internal authority**
4. **Replace rumination with pattern language**

These exercises help you practice each step until the process becomes second nature —
until your nervous system has a new route home that doesn't pass through hypervigilance
and shame.

---

### Exercise 14A: Loop Recognition Log

When the old pattern activates, something happens in your body before your mind catches
up. Your heart rate changes. Your stomach tightens. Your jaw locks. Your hands get cold.
The body knows before you do.

This log helps you catch the loop earlier and earlier, until you can name it in real
time.

**Use this log whenever you notice activation — after an interaction, a memory, a
trigger, or even a dream. The goal isn't to analyze. It's to notice.**

### Exercise 14B: "This Belongs to Then, Not Now"

Your nervous system doesn't have a calendar. When it gets activated, it doesn't know
whether the threat is happening right now or happened fifteen years ago. It just fires.

This exercise helps you translate body sensations into pattern language — to build a
bridge between what your body feels and what you now understand about where those
feelings came from.

**Instructions:** In the left column, write the body sensation or emotional reaction.
In the right column, translate it into its origin — the old dynamic it belongs to. You
don't have to get it perfect. You just have to name it.

| What I feel now | What this belongs to |
|---|---|
| *Example: Sudden shame when someone goes quiet* | *This is the punishment of silence. I learned that quiet meant I'd done something wrong. But silence is just silence.* |
| *Example: Panic when I can't reach someone* | *This is the abandonment loop. My nervous system learned that unavailability meant danger. It doesn't mean that now.* |

**After filling in the table, practice saying each translation aloud:**

"When I feel _________________, that's the old loop activating. This belongs to then,
not now. Right now, I am safe."

This isn't positive thinking. It's accurate thinking. You're teaching your nervous
system to distinguish between echo and current threat. Every time you make the
distinction, the echo gets a little quieter.

### Exercise 14D: Shame Inventory

Shame is the narcissist's most powerful tool. It gets deposited, not generated. You've
been carrying some that was never yours.

This inventory helps you sort through the shame you're holding and return what doesn't
belong to you. This isn't about being shame-free — guilt has its place when you've
genuinely violated your own values. This is about identifying the shame that was put in
you by someone else for their benefit.

**Part B: Sort the shame.**

Go back through each one and ask the Differentiation question from Chapter 15:

*"Did I do something that violates my own values? Or am I feeling bad because someone
made me feel bad?"*

Mark each shame message:

- **M** = Mine. I genuinely acted against my values. This is guilt, not shame, and I
  can make repair.
- **D** = Deposited. Someone put this in me. This was never mine to carry.
- **?** = Not sure yet. (That's okay. Sometimes it takes time to sort.)

---

**Part C: Return what isn't yours.**

For every shame message you marked **D**, use the Return practice from Chapter 15:

Close your eyes. Visualize the shame as energy — a weight, a color, a texture, whatever
feels right. Acknowledge it: "I've been carrying this. It was never mine."

Then, either aloud or silently:

**"I return this shame to its source."**

You don't have to send it with anger. You don't have to know exactly where it goes.
You're simply setting down something that was placed on your shoulders by someone who
didn't want to carry it themselves.

## Section 15: Your Scripts & Responses

*Aligned with Chapter 16 — Practical Responses*

---

Chapter 16 gave you the principles and the scripts. This section helps you make them
yours — to rehearse, customize, and internalize responses so they're available when you
need them, not just when you're reading a book.

Remember the four core principles:

1. **You can't win the argument** — stop trying to convince them
2. **Reaction is supply** — the less you give, the less they get
3. **You don't owe explanation** — brief is better
4. **Exits are power** — leaving is strategy, not failure

### Exercise 15E: When You Want to Go Back

This is the exercise you will need on the worst night.

The one where loneliness feels worse than the abuse did. Where your brain rewrites the
story into something softer — where you remember only the good days, the early days,
the version of them that made you feel chosen. Where your phone is in your hand and
their name is right there and it would be so easy to just say hi.

This exercise is for that night.

---

**Step 1: Stop. Don't contact them yet.**

You are not making a decision right now. You are pausing. That is all. Set a timer for
72 hours. If you still want to contact them after 72 hours, you can. But not tonight.

**Step 2: Reread your own words.**

Go back to the exercises you completed in this workbook. Specifically:

- **Exercise 10B** — What I Called It Then vs. What I Call It Now
- **Exercise 14D** — Your Shame Inventory (the "D" items — the shame they deposited)
- **Exercise 14C** — Your Three Truths About You

Read what you wrote in your own handwriting. Let your past self talk to your present
self. They were clearheaded when they wrote those words. Trust them.

**Step 3: Name what you are actually feeling.**

"I want to go back" is rarely the real feeling. Underneath it, there is usually
something more specific. Check what is true right now:

- [ ] I am lonely, and I am confusing loneliness with love

### Exercise 16C: "I Believe Myself" — Somatic Exercise

This is a body exercise, not a thinking exercise. Don't skip the physical parts.

The narcissistic dynamic trained you to distrust your own perception. This exercise is
about re-establishing the connection between what you feel and what you believe about
what you feel. It's about ending the second-guessing.

---

**Step 1: Ground.**

Stand up if you're able. Feet flat on the floor, shoulder-width apart. If standing
isn't accessible, sit with your feet flat and your spine supported. Feel the ground
beneath you. You are here. You are in this room. You are in this body.

**Step 2: Breathe.**

Three slow breaths. In for four counts. Out for eight. Let the exhale be longer than
the inhale. Let your shoulders drop. Let your jaw soften.

**Step 3: Place your hand on your chest.**

Feel your heartbeat. This heart has kept you alive through everything. It didn't stop
when things were at their worst. It's still going.

**Step 4: Say these words out loud. Slowly.**

"I believe myself."

Pause. Let it land.

"I trust what I know."

Pause.

"My perception is valid."

Pause.

"I don't need anyone else to confirm what I experienced."

Pause.

"I know what happened. And I believe myself."

---

**Step 5: Notice.**

What happened in your body when you said those words?

- [ ] I felt resistance — inner critic arguing, saying "but..."
- [ ] I felt relief — something softened or released
- [ ] I felt nothing — numbness or flatness
- [ ] I felt strong — something shifted into place
- [ ] Other: _______________________________________________

---

**If resistance came up:** That's normal. The inner critic that says "but what if you're
wrong?" is the internalized voice of someone who needed you to doubt yourself. Notice
it. Name it. And choose to believe yourself anyway. Not because you're always right
about everything — nobody is. But because your experience of your own life is valid.
You were there. You felt it. You know.

**If numbness came up:** That's also normal. Dissociation was a survival strategy. Your
body learned to disconnect from feeling because feeling wasn't safe. The numbness isn't
permanent. It will thaw in its own time. Keep doing the exercise. Even if you don't
feel it yet, your nervous system is hearing you.

**Do this exercise daily for a week.** Same words. Same physical practice. Notice how
it changes over time. The first day might feel mechanical. By day seven, your body
will know the words before your mouth says them.
