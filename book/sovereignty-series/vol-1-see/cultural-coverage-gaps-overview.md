# Cultural Coverage Gaps — Overview & Integration Map

*An index to eight cultural integration proposals for Vol 1: SEE, and the cross-cutting material that ties them together.*

---

## Why This Document Exists

The original voice of Vol 1: SEE is Western, white, middle-class, and individualist. The Hispanic and LGBTQ+ integration proposals began correcting that blind spot. This year, we're extending that work with six additional proposals covering Black, Asian, Indigenous, Middle Eastern/SWANA, high-control religious, and trans-specific communities.

This overview file is the scaffolding. It holds the shared thesis, the intersectionality map, the combined integration table, and the sensitivity review requirements — so the individual proposals can stay focused on their specific community without repeating cross-cutting material. Think of this as the coordinator; the eight proposals are the specialists.

The goal is *woven integration*: no separate "cultural section," but cultural material threaded throughout the book's existing chapters, so a reader sees themselves in the text without having to hunt for a dedicated aside.

---

## The Shared Thesis

> *Narcissism adapts to whatever community you can't afford to leave.*

The wolf wears different clothing in different communities. The teeth don't change.

This is the unifying metaphor across all eight proposals. Narcissism is not a standalone pathology; it is opportunistic. It finds and exploits whatever cultural, religious, identity-based, or family structure the survivor cannot afford to leave — and then weaponizes the values of that structure against them. The structure itself is not the problem. The narcissist's exploitation of it is. A survivor in a Hispanic family may be trapped by familismo and respeto. A Black survivor by the "Strong Black Woman/Man" archetype and the unwritten rule not to "air dirty laundry." A trans survivor by medical gatekeeping and dependence on the abuser for affirmation. A religious survivor by scriptural authority and fear of hell. An Indigenous survivor by tribal belonging and the internalized message that community survival is an individual duty.

The woven-integration approach means we don't write these as add-ons. We rewrite the book's existing chapters to name, honor, and address these specific cultural vectors *where they belong* — in the analysis of vulnerability, in the naming of isolation tactics, in the roadmap to recovery, and in the validation that survivors from these communities aren't broken; they're navigating both narcissism and the legitimate community bonds that the narcissist is weaponizing.

## Cross-Community Intersectionality Map

### Where the Communities Overlap

Intersectionality isn't an addendum. A survivor's experience of narcissistic abuse is shaped by every identity they hold, and often by how those identities interact in ways that no single proposal alone can capture. Writers and reviewers must keep these intersections in mind:

- **A Black trans woman** → Black + LGBTQ+ + Transgender (state violence, medical racism, respectability pressure, family loss)
- **A queer Latino raised Catholic** → Hispanic + LGBTQ+ + Religious (familismo, shame, scriptural authority, coming-out cost within faith)
- **A second-generation Indian American leaving an arranged marriage** → Asian (South) + (possibly) Religious (Hindu, Sikh, Muslim)
- **A Mormon woman from Utah** → Religious (LDS) + (if applicable) LGBTQ+ or Transgender
- **A Palestinian Christian woman in diaspora** → Middle Eastern + Religious (Catholic or Orthodox) + (possibly) Immigrant/diaspora dynamics
- **A Native woman who converted to Evangelical Christianity** → Indigenous + Religious (Evangelical) + cultural navigation
- **An Afro-Latino survivor** → Hispanic + Black (dual marginalization, dual community pressures)
- **A Muslim Black American** → Black + Middle Eastern/Islamic (religious authority, state surveillance, community policing)
- **A trans South Asian man whose parents arranged a marriage** → Asian + LGBTQ+ + Transgender + (possibly) Religious
- **A gay Evangelical pastor's son** → LGBTQ+ + Religious (Evangelical) + religious authority dynamics

These are not additive. A Black trans woman isn't "Black" plus "trans" — her specific experience of narcissistic abuse is shaped by both at once, and often by how they interact in ways that multiply the isolation. Editors and sensitivity reviewers must watch for intersectional blind spots: patterns that look "cultural" but are actually gendered; patterns that look "religious" but are shaped by racialization; trans-specific vulnerabilities that compound in communities with strong collective identity.

## Cross-Cutting Sensitivity Review Requirements

The sensitivity review process is not optional. It is also not uniform. Each community brings its own expertise and veto power. This section outlines the minimum structure.

### Community-Specific Reviewers

Each of the eight proposals specifies its own community-specific review requirements (see individual files). In brief:

- **Hispanic:** Latino/a/x reviewers, ideally multiple countries/regions of origin; attention to gender roles within the culture
- **LGBTQ+:** Queer, lesbian, gay, bisexual, asexual, and aromantic reviewers; generational diversity (young people in the community now)
- **Black:** Black readers and writers; attention to anti-Black narratives; centering Black agency and joy
- **Asian:** Pan-Asian and diaspora-specific (East, South, Southeast); immigrant and second-generation
- **Indigenous:** Indigenous-led review is non-negotiable; if Indigenous reviewers say no, honor it; this is not about appearing diverse
- **SWANA:** Middle Eastern, North African, South Asian Muslim reviewers; awareness of Islamophobia and orientalism
- **Religious:** Ex-members and current members from each tradition (Evangelical, Mormon, JW, Catholic); attention to whether critique is accurate or strawman
- **Transgender:** Trans writers and readers; attention to medical, legal, and safety specifics; no cisgender gatekeeping of trans narratives

### Intersectional Reviewers

Additionally, recruit at least 2-3 reviewers who sit at multiple intersections (e.g., a Black queer woman, a Muslim trans man, a Latina Catholic with a disabled child, a Native LGBTQ+ person). Their role is to catch what single-community reviewers miss: the way racism compounds homophobia, the way religious control shapes gender-based abuse differently for trans people, the way diaspora adds layers to cultural isolation.

### Sensitivity Process Principles

- **Lived experience first:** Reviewers should have lived experience in the communities they're reviewing, not just academic knowledge
- **Compensation:** Pay reviewers. This is expert labor. Do not ask for free emotional labor
- **Right to veto:** If a reviewer says a section perpetuates harm, the burden is on the author to fix it, not on the reviewer to explain why they're being "too sensitive"
- **Iteration:** Allow time for multiple rounds of feedback and revision. This is not a one-pass process
- **No performative diversity:** Tokenism is worse than nothing. If you can't honor a reviewer's feedback, say so upfront; don't include them as cover for lazy integration

## Implementation Strategy

The woven-integration approach means cultural material is embedded in every chapter, not cordoned off. It means a reader should see themselves in the text without having to hunt for a dedicated section. It requires coordination, iteration, and genuine collaboration with community reviewers.

### Phase 1: Review & Alignment

- **Author review of all eight proposals together:** Before beginning to draft integration, read all eight proposals as a whole. Note where they overlap, where they contradict, where they speak to the same dynamic differently. Build your mental model of the landscape
- **Community-specific sensitivity review:** Circulate all eight proposals (or individual ones as completed) to relevant reviewers. This isn't final approval; it's feedback on accuracy, emphasis, and potential harm
- **Intersectional review:** Bring together 2-3 intersectional reviewers. Ask them: where do these proposals miss each other? What compound effects aren't being named?
- **Reconcile conflicts:** If proposals disagree (e.g., on how to frame religious authority, or filial duty, or identity), document that disagreement in this overview and note it for editors. Cultural frameworks are not monolithic; survivors in the same culture may have different experiences

### Phase 2: Drafting Order

- **Start with high-impact chapters:** Chapters 7, 8, 9, 10, and 16 are where cultural material lands hardest. Draft integration there first
- **Vary which community anchors each field note:** Don't let one community dominate the examples. Rotate so Chapter 7 field note might be Black, Chapter 8 might be Asian, Chapter 10 might be Indigenous, etc.
- **Leave space for detail:** Integration doesn't mean everything at once. A Hispanic-centered field note doesn't need to also acknowledge all eight communities. The table above shows what *can* be added; pick the most relevant, then ensure other chapters pick up the others

### Phase 3: Integration Check

- **Verify woven integration, not special sections:** Search the draft for any paragraph that reads like "and here's the diverse version." If found, rewrite it. The cultural material should feel as native to the chapter as the default examples
- **No community feels like a token add-on:** Read through the book and count how many times each community is named, in what context, with what agency. If one community is mentioned twice and another ten times, that's a data point for rebalancing
- **Ensure variation in how each community appears:** Not every community in every chapter; but across the book, every community should have moments of depth, not just surface reference

### Phase 4: Final Sensitivity Pass

- **Full-text review with all original reviewers:** Return the completed integration to reviewers. Are they represented as they asked? Are stereotypes gone? Is critique accurate?
- **Intersectional review again:** The intersectional reviewers should also review the full book to catch things that single-community reviewers might miss
- **Veto window:** Reviewers should have the right to request changes before publication. If they don't grant permission, the material doesn't ship

## What This Is NOT

This document is scaffolding, not the final product. Be clear about its limits:

- **Not a replacement for the individual proposals.** This overview coordinates them; it does not substitute for their depth. Writers must read all eight
- **Not a ranked hierarchy of communities.** None of these communities is more important than another. The order here is arbitrary
- **Not exhaustive.** African continental survivors, East European survivors, Jewish survivors, Caribbean survivors, disability-identified survivors, neurodivergent survivors, elderly survivors, incarcerated survivors — these communities all have distinct narcissistic abuse patterns. This is Vol 1. Future work is required
- **Not a license to skip reviewers.** An overview that says "we're doing intersectional work" is not a substitute for actual community review
- **Not an excuse for lazy integration.** "We mentioned them in the overview" does not count as woven integration. Integration means rewriting chapters, not adding footnotes
- **Not a finished editorial product.** This is the scaffolding. Editors will add their own work on top of it

## Communities Not Yet Covered

Narcissism adapts to every community. The book's future editions should integrate proposals from survivors in these communities:

- **African continental diaspora** (distinct from African American; includes recent immigrants, refugees, and their children)
- **East European / Slavic** (Soviet legacy, post-Soviet migration, specific honor/shame dynamics, Orthodox Christianity)
- **Jewish** (Orthodox, Hasidic, secular, diasporic; religious authority, tikkun olam and guilt, cultural trauma, historical safety concerns)
- **Caribbean** (colonialism, linguistic dynamics, family structures, diaspora, spiritual traditions)
- **Disability and chronic illness** (medical control, dependence, iatrogenic harm, community barriers)
- **Neurodivergent** (ADHD, autism, dyslexia; pathologization, internalized shame, misdiagnosis as narcissism, communication differences)
- **Elderly survivors** (financial exploitation, isolation, medical abuse, cognitive decline concerns)
- **Incarcerated and formerly incarcerated** (state control, abuser-as-state, reentry, community judgment)

Each of these communities has earned the same depth of work as the eight proposals here. They should be future priorities.

## Closing

The goal of woven integration is simple: a survivor should see themselves in the book without having to flip to a "special section." The cultural material isn't an add-on. It's part of the book's DNA.

This means the book itself changes. Chapters are rewritten, not supplemented. Field notes draw from different cultural contexts. The examples, the language, the framework all shift to hold more than one world at a time.

This is hard work. It requires writers to learn. It requires reviewers to invest time and care. It requires editors to say no to shortcuts and yes to iteration. But the payoff is a book that doesn't ask survivors to translate themselves to be seen. A book that names the wolf in every disguise.

*Narcissism adapts to whatever community you can't afford to leave. The wolf wears different clothing in different communities. The teeth don't change. This book should name every disguise.*
