## Overview

I'm Sage. I read for representation. Round 5 closed 22 decisions and the
implementation landed earlier today (see
`review-05/phase-2-content/sage-implementation-log.md`). This refresh is my
second-pass read of the resulting prose.

Most of the additions fit. A handful of places warrant a second look —
either because the added passages may read as bolted on, or because the
current manuscript surfaces concerns that Round 5 didn't name. Twelve
questions below, organized into three categories: **Integration Quality**
(does the new content fit the voice?), **Remaining Round 5 Gaps**, and
**New Observations** from the current read.

**Why this phase matters:** Additions made to please a sensitivity review
sometimes read as disclaimers pasted into the flow. When that happens,
readers from the represented groups often sense the performance and feel
*more* othered, not less. The goal of this refresh is to confirm the
newly-added passages actually serve the readers they name.

**Editor Comments:**

- [ ] B) Echo a short version in Lesson 1.1 after "You are not alone" (30–50 words)

## CATEGORY C: New Observations From Current Read

### Q9: "Jennifer's story" pattern and female-coded victim defaults

**Editor Comments:**
Across lessons, when a named or lightly-sketched victim example appears,
the default seems to lean female — "the woman who finally broke free," "she
gradually cut her off," "her job," etc. The Round 5 work added male examples
in the reproductive coercion section and the 1-1 callout, which is good. But
the baseline pattern of anonymous-example-defaults-to-female is still
detectable. For male readers, a lot of "you" text is gender-neutral, but
the specific anchor examples they can picture still skew female-survivor. A
reader who doesn't identify with the default examples has to do extra
imaginative work each time.

**The Question:** Should we deliberately balance anonymous-example defaults?

**Options:**
- [ ] A) Audit 5–10 anonymous examples across lessons and flip roughly half
  to male-coded defaults
- [ ] B) Add a front-matter author note explaining the example balance is
  intentionally varied and not prescriptive
- [ ] C) Leave as-is; the "For Men Reading This" callout handles it
- [ ] D) Author to decide on a case-by-case basis as other revisions come up

**Recommendation:** A + D. A small audit pass plus ongoing awareness in
future edits. A full overhaul isn't necessary; a visible-enough balance is.

**Clarifying question:** Would you like me to produce the audit list (which
5–10 examples and what the coin-flip outcome would be), or handle it in-line

### Q10: "The narcissist" as a noun throughout

**Editor Comments:**
Throughout the course, "the narcissist" and "narcissists" appear frequently
as nouns identifying a class of people. Examples: "The narcissist uses…",
"narcissists often…", "when your parent is the narcissist." The Round 5
Q15 disclaimer about pattern recognition vs. diagnosis is now in 1-1 (good),
but the body of the manuscript still uses the diagnostic noun freely. Some
survivors find the noun clarifying; others (particularly those working on
radical acceptance without diagnostic labeling, and clinicians' concerns
about pathologizing) find the noun harder to hold.

**The Question:** Revisit the noun usage, or trust the Lesson 1.1 frame to
carry the whole course?

**Options:**
- [ ] A) Trust the Lesson 1.1 disclaimer; the noun is the working shorthand
- [ ] B) Add a one-paragraph note in Lesson 2.1 after the playbook concept
  explicitly saying "when we say 'the narcissist' we mean the person
  operating from this playbook, diagnosed or not"
- [ ] C) Do a pass to replace "the narcissist" with "the person using these
  tactics" in a subset of places where the diagnostic weight matters most
- [ ] D) Combination: B + selective C in the most loaded lessons (3-3
  parental, 3-4 romantic)

**Recommendation:** B. Lowest-effort, high-clarity fix. Option C risks
making the prose clinical and cumbersome.
